# 8. The money

**What this chapter settles:** how to recognise the money in a furnished restaurant without confusing it with the things around it.

## Money is anything that costs or earns

Money is like the fuel gauge on a long car journey: ignore it and you stop somewhere
inconvenient. **Roughly nine pence in every pound leaves an average kitchen unrecorded.**

**If the restaurant must pay for it, it costs. If it brings payment into the restaurant, it earns. Either answer makes it money.**

This includes the till and the takings. It includes what an ingredient costs to use. It also includes what the owner is willing to spend in a day. That amount is a cost the owner has allowed, even before all of it has been spent.

One test covers all four. It keeps money from changing its name merely because it appears in a different part of the restaurant.

## Cost tracking

An ingredient and its cost are not the same thing. **The ingredient is stock; what it costs to use is money.** Putting the cost with the ingredient would merge two categories and make neither one dependable.

Use the same care elsewhere. A door may be part of the building. A chair may be furniture. A knife may be a utensil. An oven may be equipment. The amount any of them costs is money, but the thing itself keeps the category that describes what it is.

Ask about the exact thing in front of you. Do not move a thing into money merely because it has a price beside it. Move the price.

## The money test takes the till first

The till can look like equipment because it may be powered and may do work for you. **The money test takes priority for the till.** Its purpose is to receive the takings, so it belongs with money and is not counted again as equipment.

This order matters. Without it, one restaurant would place the till with equipment and another would place it with money. The furnished buildings would already disagree before a dish was made.

## The owner's daily amount is a decision already made

What the owner is willing to spend in a day belongs with money. It sets the cost the restaurant may take on that day.

**The owner decides the amount; the restaurant does not send the owner work to discover it.** Once decided, the amount is simply part of the money that exists in the furnished building.

## Money exists before anyone handles it

This chapter puts money in the furnished building. It does not say who handles it or what they do with it.

**Part Two says that money exists. Part Three says how it is handled.** Keeping those promises apart prevents a category from quietly becoming a recipe.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Count as money anything that costs or earns.
2. Include the till, the takings, what an ingredient costs to use and what the owner is willing to spend in a day.
3. Ask about the exact thing being sorted. Keep an ingredient as stock and its cost as money.
4. Keep the building, furniture, utensils and equipment separate from the money they cost.
5. Apply the money test to the till before the equipment test. Count the till as money even when it is powered, and do not count it again as equipment.
6. Treat what the owner is willing to spend in a day as a decision already made. Never turn it into work sent to the owner.
7. State that money exists in the furnished building. Leave how it is handled to Part Three.
