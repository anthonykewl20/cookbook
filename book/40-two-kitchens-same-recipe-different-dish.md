# 40. Two kitchens, same recipe, different dish

**What this chapter settles:** how comparing two restaurants reveals what a shared recipe failed to say, and how that finding changes the manual.

## One kitchen cannot prove its recipe is complete

A restaurant follows a recipe and produces one dish. That result may be good every time.
It still cannot show that the recipe contains every instruction.

The same chefs may be supplying a missing step from memory. They may all assume an
ingredient, or use a temperature nobody wrote down. The dish appears to prove the recipe
works, when it may only prove that this shift knew what the recipe forgot.

**One restaurant has one result and nothing to compare it with.** It cannot tell which part
came from the writing and which part came from remembered knowledge.

## Two kitchens reveal the missing words

Before comparing the dishes, confirm that both kitchens are working from exactly the same
wording. If one has older wording, stop. The difference does not measure the recipe. It
shows that one kitchen missed an amendment, which is the fault chapter 39 handles.

Once the wording matches, let both kitchens follow every step they can read. If their dishes
differ, the difference did not appear from nowhere. One kitchen supplied something the
recipe never said, or the words allowed more than one result.

That is why **a chain is a measuring instrument for the manual.** The second kitchen does
more than make another dish. It reveals the instructions the first kitchen never knew it
was remembering.

Remembered knowledge dies with the shift that holds it. Written knowledge remains for the
next stranger. Only comparison across kitchens exposes the gap while there is still a
result on each side to show it.

## Two followed processes put the fault in the writing

Do not decide that the second kitchen is wrong merely because its dish is different. Read
each restaurant's own log. **Instructions travel. Records never do.** The logs remain
separate, but each one shows what its kitchen did and whether it followed the written
process.

When both did, the verdict is plain: **the recipe is wrong, not the chef.** The writing
allowed two kitchens to obey it and still produce different dishes. This is the four-way
verdict at chain scale: process followed in both places, results differ, so the fault is in
the writing.

Blaming either chef would hide the useful finding. The chain has found a sentence the
manual needs but does not yet contain.

## The difference passes through the one door

A missing instruction does not become a private correction. It becomes a fault.

1. Raise the difference as a fault from below.
2. Write the differing results and the missing or unclear instruction into the operations
   log of the restaurant that raises it.
3. Report the written fault upward.
4. Let the owner decide what the recipe must say.
5. Amend the manual once, for every restaurant.

Until the owner decides, the kitchens do not fill the gap by changing their own copies.
The comparison has measured the manual. It has not given either kitchen permission to
rewrite it.

## Never fix the recipe in only one kitchen

The tempting answer is to add the missing step where the difference appeared. It feels
quick, and the next dish there may improve.

Do not do it. **A private improvement destroys the comparison that exposed the fault.** It
also leaves the chain with two written promises. Record the fault, send it upward and wait
for one decision that amends the recipe for everybody.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Compare the results of at least two restaurants following the same written recipe, and
   use that comparison to measure whether the recipe is complete.
2. Never treat one restaurant's result as proof that its recipe contains every instruction.
3. Confirm that both kitchens are working from exactly the same wording before comparing
   their dishes. If one has older wording, stop and treat that as a missed amendment, not a
   fault in the recipe.
4. Read each restaurant's own log and establish that both kitchens followed the written
   process before judging why their dishes differ. Keep the logs separate.
5. When both kitchens followed the process but their dishes differ, find the fault in the
   recipe and never blame either chef.
6. Treat every assumed step, ingredient or temperature exposed by the comparison as missing
   writing, not as knowledge the next shift may be expected to remember.
7. Raise the difference as a fault from below. Write it into the operations log of the
   restaurant that raises it, report it upward, obtain the owner's decision and amend the
   manual once for every restaurant.
8. Never repair one kitchen's copy of the recipe.
