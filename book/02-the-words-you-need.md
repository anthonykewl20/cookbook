# 2. The handful of words you need before you can talk at all

**What this chapter settles:** what six of your kitchen's first agreed words mean, so nobody mistakes one person, the machine or one piece of work for another.

## Chapter 1 chose the words and this chapter fixes their meanings

Chapter 1 explains how you arrive at your own words. **You may choose any words that feel natural, but the six meanings below stay fixed.** Otherwise two kitchens could sound alike while expecting different things.

Write your choices down in the kitchen where they were agreed. Keep this book blank on those choices, because another owner may choose different words for the same meanings.

## Your owner word names the person with the final say

The first word means you: the person who owns the place. **The owner makes the decisions that belong to the whole place or chain.** Ownership does not mean doing every part of the work or answering every question.

The owner may own one restaurant or the whole chain. **This is the person whose decision is final.** Nobody else quietly makes an owner's choice instead.

## Your day-to-day word names the person who keeps one place running

The second word means the person who runs one restaurant from day to day. **They act within the decisions already made.** They settle ordinary questions and make sure each piece of work has someone responsible for it.

This person does not turn their own work into the owner's work. When the owner's attention is truly needed, what goes upward is a decision for the owner to make, never a task for the owner to perform.

## Your work word names the people who make what was asked for

The third word means the people who do the work. **It names the people responsible for producing whatever your agreed unit-of-work word names.** It never names the thing they produce.

It does not mean the owner merely because the owner asked. It does not mean the checker merely because checking is also work. Each word answers a different question: who owns, who runs, who makes and who checks.

## Your checking word names the person who judges the work

The fourth word means the person who checks what the workers made. **That person decides whether what your agreed unit-of-work word names is acceptable; the maker does not give that verdict.**

A check made by the same hands that made the work is only the maker's opinion repeated. This word covers the one checking responsibility agreed here. Other responsibilities can be named later, when they are needed.

## Your machine word names the one appliance everyone works on

The fifth word means the machine on which the kitchen's work is done. **It names the one appliance everyone works on, and only that appliance.**

The appliance is none of the people named above. It is the machine they work on. Giving it one clear name lets everyone know which machine a conversation is about.

## Your unit-of-work word names the thing that moves from maker to checker

The sixth word means one piece of output: **the single thing the workers produce and the checker judges.** It is the object that moves from the person who makes it to the person who checks it.

This book may call that object a dish when it uses its own restaurant picture. That is the book's example, not your settled term. In your kitchen, use the local word you agreed and keep that choice out of this book.

Use that word for one unit of work only. Never use it for a person, the appliance or a responsibility. Keeping the object separate from its maker and checker makes every handover clear.

## Never send the owner unfinished work to perform

It is tempting for the person running the place to pass difficult work upward and call that ownership. Do not. **Send the owner only a decision that only the owner can make, never a task to perform.**

If every restaurant sends its unfinished work to the owner, the owner ends up running every restaurant day to day and cannot own the chain.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Use the locally agreed word for the person who owns the place and has the final say.
2. Use the locally agreed word for the person who keeps one place running day to day.
3. Send the owner decisions that only the owner can make. Never send the owner a task to perform.
4. Use the locally agreed work word for the people who produce whatever the locally agreed unit-of-work word names.
5. Use the locally agreed checking word for the person who checks whether the thing named by the locally agreed unit-of-work word is acceptable.
6. Never let the maker give the checking verdict on the same unit of work they made.
7. Use the locally agreed machine word for the one appliance on which everyone works.
8. Use the locally agreed unit-of-work word for the single thing that moves from maker to checker. Never use it for a person, the appliance or a responsibility.
9. Keep all six meanings separate. Never use one agreed word for another meaning.
10. Write the six agreed words down only in the kitchen where they were chosen. Never write them into this book.
