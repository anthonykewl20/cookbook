# 41. When one restaurant drifts

**What this chapter settles:** how to tell when one restaurant has departed from a sound shared manual, and how to bring it back.

## Drift puts the fault in one restaurant

The manual can be sound while one restaurant stops following it. That is chain-level drift.
**The restaurant has changed the promise for itself, while the rest of the chain still keeps
the shared promise.**

This is the opposite of the finding in the previous chapter. When two kitchens both follow
the process and their dishes differ, the manual failed to say enough. When one restaurant
does not follow the process, the fault is in that restaurant. Do not amend a sound recipe to
explain a kitchen that did not obey it.

## The log shows where practice left the writing

Instructions say what should happen. The operations log says what did happen. **Comparing
the two is the sharpest place drift shows, not the only place.** If the recipe requires the
taster's verdict before service but the log shows service came first, the disagreement is
already enough. You do not need a second restaurant to prove it. A logged removal from the
menu is a local record of which recipes this restaurant currently serves, explained below.

The chain gives you another view. The area manager may find that one restaurant's log
disagrees with the shared instructions while the others do not. Dishes may also differ
because that kitchen alone skipped or changed the process. The area manager reports the
pattern. It does not taste the dishes or give the verdict.

## One comparison separates drift from a missing instruction

Read the instruction beside the restaurant's log first.

1. If this restaurant departed from the writing, it drifted. No second restaurant is needed.
2. If it followed the writing but its dish differs, compare it with another kitchen. If both
   followed, the fault is in the manual, as chapter 40 explains.

**A different dish is evidence, not the verdict.** The logs decide whether one kitchen left
the process or whether the shared writing left something unsaid.

## A shorter menu keeps the shared promise

A restaurant may remove a chain dish from its menu and add that removal to its log. That is
the one local right, not drift. It may serve fewer dishes, never different ones.

The recipe is never local. Neither are the house rules, the taster's checklist or any job
description. **Rewriting any of those in one restaurant is drift**, even when the reason is
sensible and the dish improves.

## The restaurant returns to the manual

Drift is not cured by improving the restaurant's private wording. That would leave two
promises in place. **Remove the local rewrite of the travelling instruction and return the
restaurant to the shared instruction.** Add what happened next to the end of its log; never
rewrite what the log already recorded.

If the manual itself needs changing, use its one door. Staff raise the fault from below, add
it to the log and report it upward. You decide, and the manual is amended once for
everybody. Drift means the restaurant was wrong, so the first answer is return, not
amendment.

## Never accept a private improvement

A change can look harmless when it produces a better dish. That is what makes it tempting.

Written instructions travel unchanged. Do not allow a restaurant to rewrite one on arrival
or during service. **Anything that changes how a dish is cooked without passing through the
one door is drift.** A restaurant that does this is outside the chain, because it is no
longer keeping the chain's promise.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Treat one restaurant's departure from a sound shared manual as chain-level drift.
2. Compare the restaurant's instructions with its operations log first. Treat their
   disagreement as drift and as the sharpest place drift shows, not the only place. Do not
   treat a logged menu shortening as drift.
3. Use cross-restaurant reading to find where one restaurant departed from instructions.
   Have the area manager report the pattern; never let it taste dishes or give the verdict.
4. When one restaurant did not follow the process, put the fault in that restaurant. When
   both restaurants followed it and their dishes differ, put the fault in the manual.
5. Allow a restaurant to shorten its menu and add the removal to its log. Never call that
   drift, and never allow it to serve a different dish.
6. Treat any local rewrite of a recipe, house rule, taster's checklist or job description as
   drift.
7. Remove every local rewrite of a travelling instruction, return the restaurant to the
   shared instruction and add the return to the end of its log. Never rewrite the log.
8. Change the manual only through its one door: raise the fault from below, add it to the
   log, report it upward, obtain the owner's decision and amend the manual once for
   everybody.
9. Never allow a restaurant to rewrite a travelling instruction on arrival or during
   service. Treat any restaurant that does so as outside the chain.
