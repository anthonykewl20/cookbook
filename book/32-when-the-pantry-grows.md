# 32. When the pantry grows

**What this chapter settles:** when pantry work needs another pair of hands, and what must be written before they can work without colliding.

## A fuller pantry is not the strain

More ingredients do not by themselves require more people. A large pantry can still be kept
in view by one pair of hands. **Growth begins when someone's attention runs out, not when
the pile grows.**

The strain may fall on the cook or prep cook who can no longer hold both preparation and
the pantry work in view. It may fall on the head chef who can no longer keep the pantry and
its current stocktake in view while running the kitchen. The sign is the same: something
that should be noticed disappears from attention while the other work is being done.

## The pantry already belongs to this restaurant

The building was furnished in chapter 3, and chapter 7 placed its stock inside it. Growth
does not create a new kind of thing. It only puts more load on work the restaurant already
has.

**The stocktake from chapter 11 is this restaurant's local photograph.** It says what this
building has now. It is not an instruction for another restaurant and never travels there.
The written pantry job is different: it says what should be done, so a stranger can do it.

## The work is written before it is divided

Nothing may be handed to someone else until it is written down well enough that a stranger
could do it. **The written instruction for the larger pantry comes before the extra pair of
hands, never after.**

Write down every pantry action being divided, the order it happens in, where each person's
responsibility begins and ends, and when it passes to the next role. Prep already follows
this rule: every step is in the recipe from day one, while the cook does the work until
attention forces the split.

If the next worker would still need an explanation from the present one, the job is not
ready. Raise the missing instruction as a fault, add it to the end of the log and report it
upward. The owner decides whether the manual changes, once for everybody.

## Separate hands need separate work and clear responsibility

Before another pair of hands enters, the doorman must keep each worker at the proper
separate bench. **The written instruction must give one role and shift responsibility for
each pantry job at a time, before either worker begins.** It must also say when that
responsibility passes, so neither worker repeats, moves or overlooks the other's work.

The log records what actually happened and which role and shift did it. It is added to at
the end and never rewritten. The instruction prevents a collision; the log makes any
collision visible afterwards. The stocktake does neither job. It remains the local
photograph, not a travelling instruction or a diary.

## Growth adds hands without changing the floor

The new pair of hands may wear the existing prep cook or cook hat required by the written
work. **This is a growth split, not an eighth base role.** The prep cook remains a growth
role whose work was already in the recipe.

The seven base roles remain. So do the head chef's method check, the taster's quality check
and the doctor's fitness check. Pantry growth replaces none of them. Nobody approves work
they made, and if a required checker is absent, the kitchen does not serve.

## Never change the recipe to suit the pantry

A local ingredient or supplier may make a recipe inconvenient. Do not quietly alter the
recipe to fit what this pantry has. **The pantry and its photograph are local; the chain's
recipe is not.** Pantry growth must organise the hands around the written dish, never change
the dish around the pantry.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Grow pantry work only when the attention of the cook, prep cook or head chef carrying it
   runs out. Never use the amount of stock or work alone as the signal.
2. Keep the pantry and its stock in the furnished building. Keep its stocktake as a local
   photograph, never as an instruction that travels.
3. Hand over pantry work only after every action, its order, its boundary and its hand-off
   are written well enough for a stranger to follow.
4. Raise a missing pantry instruction as a fault, add it to the end of the log and report it
   upward. Let the owner decide and amend the manual once for everybody.
5. Require the doorman to keep each worker at the proper separate bench.
6. Give one role and shift responsibility for each pantry job at a time before work begins,
   and state when responsibility passes.
7. Add what actually happened and which role and shift did it to the end of the log. Never
   rewrite the log or use the stocktake in its place.
8. Add another wearer of the existing prep cook or cook hat. Never create an eighth base
   role for pantry growth.
9. Keep all seven base roles and the head chef, taster and doctor checks unchanged. Never
   let a maker approve their own work, and do not serve without every required checker.
10. Never change a chain recipe to suit local stock or a local supplier.
