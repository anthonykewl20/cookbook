# 42. What is local by right

**What this chapter settles:** what one restaurant may decide for itself without weakening the chain's promise.

## The menu alone stays local

The menu is an instruction. It tells the kitchen which dishes it may serve. But it is the
one instruction that belongs to one restaurant rather than to the whole chain.

**Every other instruction travels unchanged from the chain to every restaurant.** The menu
is the sole exception. Each restaurant keeps its own, because what it can honestly offer may
be smaller than what the chain offers.

This exception stops at the edge of the menu. The recipe itself is never local. Neither are
the house rules, the job descriptions or any other instruction.

## A shorter menu keeps the promise

A restaurant may leave off a dish that the chain serves. It may not add a dish of its own,
and it may not change a chain dish. **It may serve fewer dishes than the chain, never
different ones.**

That difference is exact. A shorter menu changes what the restaurant offers. A changed
recipe changes what the chain promises. The first is a local right. The second puts a
different dish under a shared name.

## Missing fish removes the dish

Suppose one restaurant cannot get the fish named in a recipe. It does not cook a different
fish and call the result the same dish. **It takes the dish off its menu.**

The restaurant does not seek permission from the owner or the area manager. Menu shortening
is already its right. It exercises that right and records what happened.

## A recorded removal is the honest answer

The removal is added to the operations log as a recorded event. It stays visible there,
because the log says what actually happened.

An absolute ban on local variation would look stronger, but it would be broken quietly when
a restaurant could not follow a recipe. A substitute might still leave the kitchen under
the old name. The chain would then promise sameness while serving something different.

**A logged removal is honest, visible and costs the promise nothing.** Every dish still
served remains the same dish in every restaurant that serves it.

## Repeated removals reveal a shared problem

One removal may belong to one restaurant. Three restaurants dropping the same dish are not
three separate local difficulties. **They are information about that recipe.**

The area manager can see the pattern across the chain, where one kitchen can see only its own
removal. The menu stays local, but several local records can still show that the shared dish
needs attention.

## Never turn the menu into a recipe

It is tempting to treat a local menu as permission to make a local version of a dish. Do not
do it. The menu chooses which shared dishes the restaurant serves. It never changes how any
of them are made.

This is the only local right, and it is enough. **The menu is local. The recipe never is.**

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Treat the menu as an instruction and as the sole exception to travelling instructions.
2. Keep every instruction other than the menu unchanged across the chain.
3. Allow each restaurant to serve fewer chain dishes, never different dishes.
4. When a restaurant cannot follow a dish's recipe, remove that dish from its menu. Never
   substitute an ingredient or change the recipe locally.
5. Exercise the right to shorten a menu without seeking permission from the owner or the
   area manager.
6. Add every menu removal to that restaurant's operations log as a recorded event.
7. Treat the same dish being removed by three restaurants as information about the shared
   recipe, and let the area manager see the pattern across the chain.
8. Keep every recipe unchanged in every restaurant that serves it.
