# 26. Is everything still fit?

**What this chapter settles:** how the doctor catches decline in the people and the building, and sends a clear remedy onward without treating the fault.

## The doctor asks about the means

The restaurant has three base checks because three different things can fail. The doctor's
question is **"Is everything still fit to work?"** It asks about the means: the people who do
the work and the building they work with.

The doctor does not decide whether a recipe was followed or whether a dish is good. Those
questions belong to the other checks. It asks whether the hands, equipment and place that
made the dish are still able to do their work properly.

## Decline leaves a trail in the log

Some failures arrive slowly. A worker gets worse at a familiar task. Equipment begins to
fail. The building itself gets sicker. **The doctor reads the log for that decline**, not to
repeat the general reading of everything that happened.

A step that used to take five minutes now takes twenty. The same worker keeps fumbling the
same task. An oven runs cold. One entry may be a difficult moment. A repeated change shows
that the means may no longer be fit.

## The doctor speaks as soon as decline appears

The doctor does not save its finding for the end of the week. Waiting lets a weakening
worker or failing oven reach more dishes. **It says so at once, and names what is wrong.**

"Something is wrong" is not enough. The head chef cannot act on a warning with no fault in
it. The doctor states which role, piece of equipment or part of the building is declining,
and what the evidence shows.

## A diagnosis ends with a clear handover

The doctor's finding has three parts: the fault, the remedy, and the role that should carry
the remedy out. It hands all three to the head chef, who makes the treatment happen.

**The doctor diagnoses and prescribes. It never treats.** That keeps its hands free to watch
the whole restaurant. A doctor that begins treating one patient can only hold that patient,
and a finder that quietly fixes faults soon stops reporting the faults it fixed.

The actual treatment belongs after this handover and is dealt with in chapter 27.

## A faulty manual still uses the one door

Sometimes the worker and equipment are fit, but the diagnosis points to the manual. The
doctor does not amend it. **A fault in the manual follows the same one door as every other
fault:** it is raised in the log, reported upward, decided by the owner, and amended once for
everybody.

That keeps a sound diagnosis from becoming a private recipe in one restaurant.

## Without the doctor the restaurant cannot serve

The doctor is one of the three base checks. It is not an extra that a small restaurant may
drop. **If the doctor is absent, the restaurant has fallen below the floor: no checker, no
service.** Without this check, nobody can say the people and building are still fit.

## The owner ends the chain of checks

The doctor does not judge its own fitness, but the answer is not a fourth role inside the
restaurant. That would create another checker to check. **The owner judges the doctor from
outside the building, and the chain stops there.** When the chain grows, an area manager may
hold these chains for the owner. The doctor's own check never grows another doctor.

## Never let the doctor treat the fault

It is tempting to let the doctor fix a fault because it already knows the remedy. Do not.
The moment it treats, it loses the free hands that let it watch the whole restaurant. Name
the remedy, hand it to the head chef, and keep the doctor looking for the next decline.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Require the doctor to ask whether the people, equipment and building are still fit to
   work. Keep this check separate from checks of the method and the result.
2. Require the doctor to read the log for decline in a worker, equipment or the building.
3. Report decline at once. Name what is wrong and the evidence for it; never report only
   that something is wrong.
4. Name the fault, the remedy and the role that should carry it out, then hand all three to
   the head chef.
5. Never allow the doctor to treat a fault it diagnoses.
6. Send every fault in the manual through the one door: raise it in the log, report it
   upward, leave the decision to the owner, and amend the manual once for everybody.
7. Keep the doctor present as one of the three base checks. If it is absent, do not serve.
8. Never let the doctor judge its own fitness or add a fourth checking role. Have the owner
   judge the doctor from outside the building; allow an area manager to hold the chain as
   the chain grows.
