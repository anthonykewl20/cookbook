# 26. Is everything still fit?

**What this chapter settles:** how the doctor decides whether the people and the building are still fit, and sends a clear remedy onward without treating the fault.

## The doctor asks whether the means are still fit

The restaurant has three base checks because three different things can fail. The doctor's
question is **"Is everything still fit to work?"** It asks about the means: the people who do
the work and the building they work in, including its equipment.

The doctor does not decide whether the recipe was followed or whether the dish is good.
Those questions belong to the other two checks. It asks whether the people, equipment and
place used to make the dish can still do their work properly.

## A diagnosis is made from what is seen and what is logged

**The doctor draws on direct observation, the log, and comparison over time.** It watches
the people, equipment and place while they work. This can reveal a worker beginning to
fumble a familiar task or an oven running cold before anyone has written about it.

The log supplies what happened earlier. It includes faults already raised by other people,
because those faults sit in the same log as the rest of the restaurant's day. The doctor
reads all of this as evidence about fitness, not as a general review of the whole day.

The doctor then compares what it sees now with what happened before. A step that used to
take five minutes and now takes twenty shows change. The same worker fumbling the same task
again shows a pattern. An oven that keeps running cold points to failing equipment. **A
diagnosis names the fault that best explains the evidence, the remedy, and the role that
should carry the remedy out.**

## The doctor speaks at once and hands treatment on

The doctor does not save its finding for the end of the week. Waiting lets decline reach
more dishes. **As soon as the evidence points to a fault, it says so and says what is
wrong.** "Something is wrong" is not a diagnosis, because the head chef cannot act on a
warning with no fault named.

The doctor hands the fault, the remedy and the responsible role to the head chef. The head
chef makes the treatment happen. **The doctor diagnoses and prescribes. It never treats.**
A doctor that treats can hold only one patient at a time. Keeping its hands free lets it
watch the whole restaurant and keeps it from quietly fixing faults that should be reported.

Treatment itself belongs to chapter 27.

## A fault in the manual still uses the one door

Sometimes the diagnosis points to the manual rather than an unfit worker or failing
equipment. The doctor does not amend it. **The fault is raised in the log, reported upward,
decided by the owner, and amended once for everybody.** That keeps one restaurant from
turning a sound diagnosis into its own private recipe.

## Without the doctor the restaurant cannot serve

The doctor is one of the three base checks, not an extra for a larger restaurant. Without
it, nobody can say that the people and building remain fit. **An absent doctor takes the
restaurant below the floor: no checker, no service.**

## The owner ends the chain of checks

The doctor does not judge its own fitness. Adding a fourth role to judge it would only
create another role that needed judging. **The owner stands outside the building, judges
the doctor, and ends the chain.** When the chain grows, an area manager may hold these
chains for the owner. It never grows another doctor inside the restaurant.

## Never let the doctor treat the fault

It is tempting to let the doctor fix a fault because it already knows the remedy. Do not.
Treatment takes away the free hands that let it watch the whole restaurant. Name the
remedy, hand it to the head chef, and keep the doctor looking for decline.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Require the doctor to ask whether the people, equipment and building are still fit to
   work. Keep this check separate from checks of the method and the result.
2. Build every diagnosis from direct observation, the log, and comparisons between what is
   happening now and what happened before.
3. Include faults raised by other people when the doctor reads the log for evidence of
   decline.
4. Report a fault as soon as the evidence points to it. Name what is wrong and the evidence;
   never report only that something is wrong.
5. Name the fault, the remedy and the role that should carry it out, then hand all three to
   the head chef.
6. Never allow the doctor to treat a fault it diagnoses.
7. Send every fault in the manual through the one door: raise it in the log, report it
   upward, leave the decision to the owner, and amend the manual once for everybody.
8. Keep the doctor present as one of the three base checks. If it is absent, do not serve.
9. Never let the doctor judge its own fitness or add a fourth checking role. Have the owner
   judge the doctor from outside the building; allow an area manager to hold the chains as
   the chain grows.
