# 28. When one waiter is not enough

**What this chapter settles:** when to add another waiter, and what must be written before two waiters can share the work without serving the same order twice.

## Attention runs out before the job changes

A busy restaurant always has work waiting. That alone is not the strain. **The strain begins
when one waiter's attention can no longer hold the customers, the open orders and the dishes
waiting on the pass counter at the same time.** One of them disappears from notice while the
waiter attends to another.

That is the signal to grow. Work may pile up during a brief rush while every order remains in
sight. Adding hands for every pile would make growth a guess. Attention gives you the firmer
test: the waiter can no longer keep the whole job in view.

## The whole waiter job is already written down

Nothing may be handed to a stranger until the stranger could do it from the writing. **The
written job must come before the extra pair of hands.**

For the waiter, taking the order is already written in chapter 15. Collecting an approved dish
from the pass counter, serving it and recording the customer's answer are already written in
chapter 20. The new waiter follows those same instructions. If your restaurant relies on an
unwritten step between them, write that gap through the proper door before adding anyone.

Do not solve the strain by quietly making one waiter the order-taker and the other the server.
That would create two different jobs before either had been written for a stranger.

## Two waiters need one rule before either moves

One waiter could see which order was already in their own hands. Two waiters cannot rely on
that private knowledge. **The written instruction must make one waiter responsible for each
order at a time, and make that responsibility clear before either waiter touches it.** It must
also make clear when a finished dish has been collected from the pass counter. Otherwise both
waiters can take the same order or reach for the same dish.

Each waiter still adds stamps 1, 4 and 5 to the operations log for what actually happened:
the order was taken accurately, the dish was served, and the customer's answer was recorded.
The log remains a diary. Add to its end and never rewrite it. Those stamps preserve the truth;
the coordination instruction prevents the collision before it happens.

## Another pair of hands wears the same hat

You are adding another wearer of the waiter hat, not inventing a new role. **Both are named
waiter, never by personal name or number, and both keep the same boundary.** Neither cooks,
changes a dish or gives the taster's verdict.

The waiter remains a hat that may be worn alongside compatible work. Adding another wearer
does not turn the hat into a checking role and does not alter the separations that keep makers
and checkers apart.

## A new instruction enters through one door

The waiters may discover that the written coordination rule is missing or fails during
service. They may not repair the manual between themselves. **Raise the fault, add it to the
log, report it upward and let the owner decide.** If a change is needed, the owner amends the
manual once for everybody.

Until that instruction is written well enough for a stranger, do not add the extra waiter.
Extra hands without a shared rule multiply the collision instead of relieving the strain.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Add another waiter only when the waiter's attention can no longer hold the customers,
   open orders and dishes on the pass counter in view. Never use a pile of work alone as the
   signal.
2. Add another waiter only after the whole waiter job is written well enough for a stranger
   to perform it.
3. Keep taking orders and serving dishes as one waiter role unless a different job has first
   been written well enough for a stranger.
4. Write one coordination instruction before two waiters work together. Make one waiter
   responsible for each order at a time, make that responsibility clear before either waiter
   touches it, and make collection from the pass counter clear before another waiter reaches
   for the same dish.
5. Add stamps 1, 4 and 5 to the end of the operations log for what actually happened. Never
   rewrite the log.
6. Name both workers waiter, never by personal name or number. Keep both inside the existing
   waiter boundary and never turn either into a checker.
7. Raise a missing or failed coordination instruction as a fault, add it to the log and report
   it upward. Let the owner decide and amend the manual once for everybody.
8. Keep the extra waiter out until the waiter job and the coordination instruction are both
   written well enough for a stranger.
