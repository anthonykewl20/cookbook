# 3. The building

**What this chapter settles:** what counts as the building, so every restaurant begins with the same clear boundary around what is fixed in place.

## This chapter gives the building its first definition

This chapter gives the building its first definition. Chapter 2's opening interview settled what the people, the machine and the unit of work are called; the building was not one of those agreed words. It is the first category this part sorts. **The building is not every thing you can see in the restaurant. It is the part fixed to the ground.**

That edge matters because the cooks will not be the same next time. A category that depends on what one cook happens to call the place will drift. A test can be repeated by anyone.

## The building stays when you move premises

**The building means anything fixed to the ground that you would leave behind if you moved premises.** It may be fixed straight to the ground or fixed to the building that stands on it.

Picture the restaurant moving elsewhere. Do not ask whether the thing is useful or important. Ask whether it stays with the old premises. If it stays because it is fixed there, it belongs to the building. If it could leave with you, it does not.

## Familiar parts of the entrance show the boundary

The path to the door is part of the building because it is fixed to the ground. The door itself is part of the building because it is fixed into the opening. How the rooms are laid out is also part of the building. You would leave that arrangement behind when you moved.

The signage and the mat show why examples do not replace the test. **Signage fixed to the building belongs here; signage that could leave does not. A mat fixed in place belongs here; a loose mat does not.** The name of the thing never settles its category. Its connection to the premises does.

## Two answers settle every doubtful case

When there is doubt, ask two questions in order:

1. Is it fixed to the ground, either directly or through the building?
2. If the restaurant moved premises, would it be left behind?

Only two yes answers put the thing in this category. This keeps the boundary steady whoever is looking.

## This chapter says what exists, not what happens there

**Part Two says a thing exists. Part Three says how it is used.** This chapter therefore settles that the path, signage, mat, door and room layout may belong to the building when they pass the test. It does not decide what anyone does with them.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Put a thing in the building category only when it is fixed to the ground, directly or through the building, and would be left behind if the restaurant moved premises.
2. Ask whether the thing is fixed to the ground, then ask whether it would be left behind on a move. Require yes to both.
3. Apply the test to the path to the door, the signage, the mat, the door itself and how the rooms are laid out. Never classify any of them by name alone.
4. State what belongs to the building in Part Two. Leave how it is used to Part Three.
