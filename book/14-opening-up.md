# 14. Opening up

**What this chapter settles:** how to open every day only after the right place, the stock and the people have been checked.

## The day begins by using what exists

The restaurant is ready to work. **Opening up means checking that what is already there is ready for this day.** You are not deciding what belongs in the restaurant again.

**This part of the book is governed by one line: standards only — no blanks, no local
variation, nothing to fill in.** Every restaurant opens with the same three checks, in the
same order, because opening is true everywhere or it does not belong in this part. The
building, the stocktake and the roster now have a job to do.

## Three checks open the day

Perform these checks before any dish begins.

1. **Check the building.** Make sure the building in use is the one you mean to open. This is the building already settled in chapter 3, not a fresh account of what is inside it. If it is the wrong building, stop and use the right one before continuing.
2. **Check the stock.** Compare what the restaurant has now with the standing stocktake from chapter 11. Read the stocktake as the current answer; do not sort the stock into categories again. If the two disagree, the stocktake is no longer current. Make the answer current before continuing.
3. **Check who is here.** Confirm that all seven base roles from chapter 9 are covered: head chef, cook, taster, doctor, waiter, cashier and dishwasher.

The order matters because each answer supports the next one. You first know where the work is happening. You then know what that place has. Only then do you confirm who is present to use it.

## Count the roles rather than the names

A roster is complete only when all seven jobs are covered. **Do not mistake one person's presence for every role that person might perform.** One person may cover compatible roles, but the three separations from chapter 9 still hold: the maker never approves the dish, the doctor never treats what the doctor finds, and nobody judges themselves.

The three check roles must each be covered by a permitted pair of hands. The head chef checks the method. The taster checks the result. The doctor checks whether the people and the building are still fit to work.

## A missing checker keeps the kitchen closed

If the head chef, taster or doctor role cannot be covered without breaking a separation, the opening check has failed. **No checker, no service. The kitchen closes until the check can be made again.** Stamp the stoppage in the operations log.

This is not a delay that can be made up after a dish leaves. A later check cannot tell you that an unchecked dish was safe to serve. Opening anyway would give up the promise of the same dish every time.

## Never promise to check later

It is tempting to open when a checker is missing and promise that someone will check the work later.

Do not do it. **The check belongs before service because it is what makes service possible.** Keep the kitchen closed until every required check can be made.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Open every restaurant with the same three checks, in this order: the right building, the stock against the current stocktake, and the seven base roles.
2. Verify that the building in use is the building meant to open. Stop and use the right building if it is not.
3. Compare what the restaurant has now with the standing stocktake. Make the stocktake current before continuing if the two disagree.
4. Confirm that the head chef, cook, taster, doctor, waiter, cashier and dishwasher roles are all covered.
5. Keep the maker from approving the dish, the doctor from treating what the doctor finds, and everyone from judging themselves when one person covers more than one role.
6. Apply “No checker, no service” whenever the head chef, taster or doctor role is absent or cannot be covered without breaking a separation. Close the kitchen until the check can be made again, and stamp the stoppage in the operations log.
7. Never open on a promise to perform a required check later.
