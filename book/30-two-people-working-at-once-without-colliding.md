# 30. Two people working at once, without colliding

**What this chapter settles:** when another pair of hands can join the kitchen, and what must already be true so two workers do not spoil each other's work.

## Attention running out is the strain

A cook can have several jobs waiting and still keep the whole job in view. That pile alone is
not a reason to grow. **The strain begins when the cook cannot hold both preparing and cooking
in their attention without one of them slipping from sight.**

Perhaps the cook turns from the pan to peel the next potatoes and misses a cooking step. That
is attention running out. A brief rush is only work waiting. Add another pair of hands for the
first problem, not merely for the second.

## Writing comes before another pair of hands

Nothing is handed over until a stranger could do it from the writing. **The written
instruction comes before the extra pair of hands, never after.**

Prep is ready to hand to a prep cook only if every step is already written into the recipe as
chapter 17 requires. Cooking is ready to hand to another cook only when the recipe leads a
stranger through the whole dish. If either worker would need to ask what happens next, keep
the work with the cook until the missing instruction has been written through the proper
door.

## Separate benches must already hold

Chapter 13 already furnished the doorman and the benches. Before two chefs work at once,
**the doorman must allow each chef to work only at the proper separate bench.** The dish is
never made in the shared main kitchen, because work mixed there can no longer be kept apart.

Two chefs do not share one bench, even when they are working on the same order. Each works at
the bench allowed for their part of the work.

## One written instruction gives each dish one responsible worker

The open orders in the operations log are the kitchen's job list. When two workers must
coordinate, private memory is not enough. **A written instruction assigns one worker
responsibility for each dish or order at a time, before either worker touches it.** When
responsibility changes, the instruction must make clear when it passes before the next
worker begins.

The log then records what actually happened. Its third stamp identifies the chef by role and
shift, never by personal name. It says what they cooked, how they cooked it and how it tasted.
That makes the hands behind each dish visible, so two workers touching the same work can be
seen. The cook's taste note remains evidence, never approval.

## Another pair of hands does not remove a check

The new worker wears an existing hat: prep cook or cook. **Name the worker by that role,
never by person.** The base checks do not shrink when the kitchen grows.

The head chef still checks the process. The taster still checks quality and did not cook the
dish. The doctor still checks whether the people and building are fit. These are separate
pairs of hands. The second worker does not replace any of them, and nobody approves work they
made. If any required checker is absent, the kitchen does not serve. No checker, no service.

## Four mistakes show the split came too early

1. **Work piled up, but attention did not run out.** The strain was mistaken.
2. **The new worker has to guess.** The job was not written for a stranger.
3. **Responsibility was spoken, not written.** It leaves with the shift.
4. **A maker is borrowed as a checker.** The required separation has disappeared.

## Never share a bench for one dish

It is tempting to let two chefs crowd onto one bench for one urgent dish. Do not. The
doorman's separation is what keeps one worker's work from being mixed with another's before
it is admitted. Urgency does not make mixed work safe.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Add another pair of hands only when a worker's attention runs out. Never use a pile of
   work alone as the signal.
2. Hand over work only after its instructions are written well enough for a stranger to do
   it. Put the written instruction before the extra pair of hands, never after.
3. Hand prep to a prep cook only after every prep step is written in the recipe. Hand cooking
   to another cook only after the recipe leads a stranger through the whole dish.
4. Require the doorman to keep each chef at the proper separate bench. Never let chefs work
   in the shared main kitchen or share one bench.
5. When two workers must coordinate, assign one worker responsibility for each dish or order
   at a time in writing before either touches it. Make clear when responsibility passes
   before the next worker begins.
6. Use the open orders in the operations log as the kitchen's job list. In the third stamp,
   identify the chef by role and shift, never by personal name, and record what they cooked,
   how they cooked it and how it tasted.
7. Treat the cook's taste note as evidence, never approval.
8. Add another prep cook or cook as a wearer of an existing role. Name the worker by role,
   never by person.
9. Preserve the separate head chef, taster and doctor checks. Never let a maker approve work
   they made or use the second worker to replace a required checker.
10. Do not serve when any required checker is absent. No checker, no service.
