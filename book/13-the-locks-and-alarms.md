# 13. The locks and the alarms

**What this chapter settles:** which parts of a furnished restaurant exist to protect it even when nobody remembers to read a rule.

## Protection does not wait to be remembered

A written rule protects the restaurant only when somebody reads and follows it. Locks and alarms are different. **They protect the building whether or not a person is reading.**

Some keep the wrong person out. Some keep what matters out of sight. Some stop an unsafe dish from leaving. Some leave a safe way out. Their shared purpose is protection, and that protection does not depend on a cook's memory.

## The safe protects what must stay secret

The safe is where secrets and other valuables are protected. **What makes it a safe is not what those valuables are worth, but the protection around them.**

Without that protection, something that must stay secret could be seen or taken by someone who should not have it. The safe belongs with the locks and alarms because its purpose is to prevent that access.

## The doorman keeps cooks at separate benches

The doorman is an automatic guard, not another piece of furniture. **The doorman controls the door and refuses work in the shared main kitchen.**

Each cook has a separate bench for a dish. The doorman keeps that separation in place by allowing work only at the proper bench. This protects one cook's work from being mixed with the restaurant's shared work before it is admitted.

## Hygiene checks protect every departing dish

Before food leaves the kitchen, hygiene checks act automatically. **They protect the restaurant even when no person remembers to ask for them.**

These checks do not taste the dish and do not give a person's verdict on its quality. They are fixed protection that catches a hygiene failure before the food goes out. A taster and a hygiene check therefore do different jobs.

## The fire exit remains a protection

The fire exit protects by leaving a way out when danger is inside. **Its value is the protection it provides, not a choice made during ordinary service.**

It belongs in this category for the same reason as the safe, the doorman and the hygiene checks: the restaurant is safer because it is there.

## This chapter furnishes protection without running it

These protections are part of the furnished restaurant. **Here, they exist; this is not a procedure for using them during service.**

There is no opening routine here, no list of who handles a lock and no person's inspection to perform. Those would describe service. This chapter settles only what protection the building contains.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Treat every protection that works whether or not a person is reading as a lock or alarm.
2. Keep secrets and other valuables protected by the safe.
3. Have the doorman allow each cook to work only at a separate bench. Never allow a cook to work in the shared main kitchen.
4. Run hygiene checks automatically before any dish leaves the kitchen.
5. Keep hygiene checks separate from the taster's verdict.
6. Keep the fire exit present as a protective way out.
7. Treat these protections as furnished parts of the restaurant; do not turn their existence into a service procedure.
