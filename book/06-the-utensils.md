# 6. The utensils

**What this chapter settles:** which things in the restaurant belong with the utensils, so every restaurant sorts them the same way.

## Chapter 2 gave the restaurant the words it needs

Chapter 2 settled the words needed to talk about the restaurant. **Those words now let you decide what the kitchen contains without reopening that conversation.**

This chapter settles the next category. It says what belongs there. It does not say how anything is used.

## Utensils are picked up to do the work

**A utensil is anything picked up by hand to do the work.** The picking up matters because the thing works through the hands of a chef. The purpose matters too. A thing does not become a utensil merely because someone can lift it.

Knives, pans, plates, crates and trays all belong here. They differ in shape and size, but each is taken in hand as part of the work.

## Being movable does not make something a utensil

A chair can be picked up. That does not make it a utensil, because picking up the chair only moves it. **For a utensil, taking it in hand is part of doing the work.**

This is the line between utensils and furniture. Furniture can be carried out of the restaurant, but it normally stays in place while the work happens. A utensil is handled while the work happens.

## Four questions settle what belongs here

Ask these questions in order:

1. **Is it picked up by hand to do the work?** If not, it is not a utensil.
2. **Is it unpowered?** If it needs power and does work for you, it is equipment, not a utensil.
3. **Is it unfixed?** If it is fixed in place, it belongs with the building, not the utensils.
4. **Is it handled as part of the work?** If it is merely possible to carry it elsewhere, it may be furniture instead.

All four answers must fit. This test keeps the category steady when two restaurants own different things. The list may differ, but the decision does not.

## The category exists before any directions begin

Part Two furnishes the building. **Here, the only promise is that the utensils exist and are sorted by the same test.** Directions for handling a knife, pan, plate, crate or tray come later, because directions tell the chefs what to do.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Sort a thing as a utensil only when it is picked up by hand to do the work.
2. Include knives, pans, plates, crates and trays as utensils.
3. Exclude anything powered.
4. Exclude anything fixed in place.
5. Exclude furniture that is merely movable rather than handled as part of the work.
6. State that each utensil exists and leave all directions for its use to Part Three.
