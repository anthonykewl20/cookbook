# 17. Prep

**What this chapter settles:** how the cook gets everything ready before cooking begins, without hiding that work or requiring a separate hire.

## Prep is work before cooking begins

At the smallest scale, the cook peels their own potatoes. **Getting the ingredients ready is part of making the dish**, even though no separate prep cook has been hired.

Prep happens before cooking. It ends with the ingredients and utensils ready for the first cooking step. Chapter 18 begins there. Keeping that line clear matters because a missing preparation cannot be quietly made up halfway through cooking.

## Prep brings three written categories together

Part Two already put everything needed for prep in the restaurant. The ingredients are stock from chapter 7. The knives and pans are utensils from chapter 6. The recipe is an instruction from chapter 10: something written that tells the cook what to do before they do it.

**Prep is the cook using the recipe to get the stock and utensils ready.** None of these things is invented at the bench. The restaurant supplies them, and the recipe says what should happen to them.

## Every preparation step belongs in the recipe

The recipe must name the preparation as work in its own right. “Prepare the potatoes” is not enough. It hides the work inside a phrase that each cook may understand differently.

Before prep begins, the recipe must:

1. Name the ingredients needed for the dish.
2. Name the utensils needed to prepare them.
3. State each preparation action in the order it is performed.
4. State where prep ends and the first cooking step begins.

**Write the peeling down in full, step by step, while the cook still performs it.** The cook then follows those steps in order. Nothing is left to memory merely because the same hands prepare and cook the dish.

## Naming the work does not create another hire

Writing prep down does not add an eighth base role. The cook performs it at this scale. **The work has a name before it has another pair of hands.**

That is what lets the restaurant grow later. If volume eventually requires the work to be handed over, a stranger can follow the same steps and leave the dish unchanged. The later split belongs to Part Four. Here, the cook still does the prep.

## The cook prepares but gives no verdict

The cook is the maker during prep, just as they are during cooking. **Making something ready is not checking it.** The cook may follow the recipe and later record what they did, but may not approve their own work or give it a quality verdict.

This chapter contains no check. Checking belongs later, and whoever checks the dish did not make it.

## The recipe comes first and the log comes later

The recipe says what should happen before the cook begins. The log later records what did happen. When the third stamp is added, it includes how the dish was made, including its prep.

**Recording prep later does not move it later.** The peeling still happened before cooking; the stamp preserves the account after the work.

## Never leave prep in the cook's memory

It is tempting to leave peeling unwritten because the cook already knows how to do it. Do not. The next cook is a stranger, and an unwritten step will change as it passes from one cook to another.

Write every preparation step into the recipe from day one. Let the cook perform it until the restaurant grows enough to hand it over.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Perform every preparation step before cooking begins, and let the cook perform it at the smallest scale.
2. Name the ingredients and utensils needed for prep in the recipe.
3. Write every preparation action into the recipe in full and in order, including where prep ends and cooking begins.
4. Never leave preparation to the cook's memory or hide several actions inside a phrase such as “prepare the potatoes.”
5. Treat prep as the maker's work. Never let the cook approve it or give it a quality verdict.
6. Name prep as work in its own right without requiring a separate prep cook.
7. Keep the written prep unchanged if the work is later handed to another role.
8. Include the prep actually performed in the third log stamp after the work. Keep every prep step before cooking begins.
