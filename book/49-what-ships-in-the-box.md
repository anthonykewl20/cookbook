# 49. What ships in the box

**What this chapter settles:** which working tools arrive with the book, so its rules hold even when nobody reads them.

## The box arrives ready for service

Installing the book does more than put words on the wall. **It gives the restaurant the
working fittings those words depend on.** They are already present before the service
chapters begin.

Each chef has a separate bench. The shared main kitchen, pass counter and till are in place.
The doorman, hygiene checks, safe and fire exit are working. The stocktake and operations
log are ready to keep their two different kinds of record.

This is how the furnished restaurant from Part Two becomes the working restaurant used in
Part Three. Nothing has to be remembered into existence on the first day of service.

## The operations log is a kept ledger

The operations log is not an idea or a loose pile of notes. **It is a ledger the appliance
keeps for the restaurant.** Inside it are the order book and the decisions book. When this
book says to stamp the operations log, that ledger is where the stamp is written.

The ledger records what actually happened, in the order it happened. It is a diary. Add
each new entry at the end, and leave every earlier entry as it was. If something needs
correcting, write the correction at the end too. Otherwise the appliance could quietly
change the past that the ledger is meant to preserve.

## The photograph stays separate from the diary

The stocktake is a different kept record. **It is the restaurant's current photograph, not
its diary.** A new photograph replaces the old one whenever the answer changes.

The two records cannot be joined. The diary must keep every earlier entry, while the
photograph must discard its earlier answer. Joining them would force one of those rules to
break.

## The protections work without being remembered

The safe protects what must stay secret. The fire exit leaves a safe way out. Hygiene checks
run before a dish leaves the kitchen. **These protections act because they are fitted, not
because a chef remembers a sentence.**

The same is true of the doorman. Chapter 48 settles its full job. Here, what matters is that
it arrives at the door already working; a written reminder cannot stand in for it.

## The restaurant arrives wired together

The separate benches, shared main kitchen, pass counter and till do not arrive as names on a
shopping list. **They arrive as working parts of one restaurant.** The doorman and hygiene
checks are already connected to the places where they act. The safe and fire exit already
provide their protections.

These fittings are not optional local choices. Otherwise two restaurants could install the
same book and begin with different protections.

## Never replace a tool with words

A reminder can look like a cheaper safe, check or doorman. It is not. **Words depend on
somebody reading them; these tools do not.** Keep the working tool in place even when the
written rule sounds perfectly clear.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Provide the separate benches, shared main kitchen, pass counter, till, doorman, hygiene
   checks, safe, fire exit, stocktake and operations log as working parts of every installed
   restaurant before service begins.
2. Keep the operations log as the appliance-kept ledger containing the order book and the
   decisions book. Write each stamp there.
3. Record what actually happened in the order it happened. Add only to the end of the
   operations log; never erase, tidy or rewrite an earlier entry. Add corrections at the
   end.
4. Keep the stocktake as the restaurant's current photograph. Replace the old stocktake
   whenever the answer changes.
5. Keep the stocktake and operations log separate. Never merge the photograph with the
   diary.
6. Keep the safe protecting what must stay secret and the fire exit providing a safe way
   out.
7. Run hygiene checks before any dish leaves the kitchen.
8. Keep every shipped protection working without depending on anybody reading or
   remembering a written rule. Never replace a working tool with words.
