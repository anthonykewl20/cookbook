# 34. When one restaurant becomes two

**What this chapter settles:** when a second restaurant protects your attention, and what must be settled before it opens.

## The second restaurant begins when your attention runs out

A full order book is not the signal. Neither is a long list of jobs. **A growth step begins
when someone runs out of attention, not when work runs out.**

At this step, that someone is you. One restaurant may leave you enough room to set direction
and hold its head chef. A second can turn that into daily supervision of two places. Add more
restaurants and your attention disappears first, however much work the kitchens can handle.

Attention is the only thing in a restaurant that cannot be bought in bulk. This step exists
to protect yours.

## The manual must be ready to stand without you

Part Four has one rule: **nothing is handed to someone else until it is written down well
enough that a stranger could do it.** Opening another restaurant is the largest hand-off yet.
You are handing the running of a whole place to people who cannot depend on what you remember.

The manual must therefore be complete and static before the second restaurant opens. Every
recipe, house rule, taster's checklist and job description must say enough for a stranger to
follow it. If you still have to explain the missing parts each morning, the job is not yet
written and cannot be handed over.

Static does not mean the manual can never improve. It means neither restaurant rewrites the
shared instructions for itself. Until the owner changes the one manual for everybody, both
places work from the same shared words. The shorter local menu described below does not
change a recipe.

## Two restaurants share instructions but not a working life

Before the two places can work together without colliding, the line between them must be
clear. **Each receives the same recipes, house rules, taster's checklist and job descriptions,
unchanged.** The menu is a local record of which chain recipes each restaurant currently
serves: one restaurant may offer fewer dishes, but never different dishes, and the recipe
itself never changes locally.

Each restaurant has its own building, stock and staff. Its stocktake is a photograph of that
place alone. Its log is its own diary and begins empty. Combining either record across the
two places would make it impossible to tell what each restaurant has and what each one did.

This is only the boundary. Chapter 35 explains the chain rule behind it. Chapter 36 handles
the opening itself and the exact division between what arrives and what begins blank.

## A chain begins where one building ends

**A chain is what growth turns into when it stops fitting in one building.** The second
restaurant is therefore more than another room to supervise. It is the hand-off from growing
one restaurant to running several from one direction.

Part Five will introduce the area manager as a chain-level role, not a job inside either
building. That role will hold the head chefs so your attention can remain on direction. It
must eventually work without keeping you in the daily loop, or it has protected nothing.

## Never build the second restaurant around your daily attention

It is tempting to open first and fill the missing instructions yourself. You know the first
restaurant, so answering every question can feel quicker than finishing the manual.

Do not do it. **If the second restaurant needs your daily memory, it is consuming the very
attention this growth step is meant to save.** Finish the writing first. Open only when a
stranger can run the place from the same written promises as the first.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Open a second restaurant only when the owner's attention is running out. Never use work
   volume alone as the trigger.
2. Complete the manual before handing a restaurant to anyone else. Write every job well
   enough that a stranger can do it.
3. Keep one static manual for both restaurants. Allow only the owner to change it, once for
   everybody.
4. Give both restaurants the same recipes, house rules, taster's checklist and job
   descriptions unchanged.
5. Allow a restaurant to shorten its menu, but never to add a different dish or change a
   recipe locally.
6. Keep each restaurant's building, stock, staff, stocktake and log separate. Start the new
   restaurant's log empty.
7. Treat the second restaurant as the beginning of a chain, not as another building for the
   owner to supervise each day.
