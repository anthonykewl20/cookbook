# 38. The area manager

**What this chapter settles:** how one chain-level role protects your attention and turns every restaurant's week into decisions you alone must make.

## The area manager belongs above every restaurant

The area manager belongs to the chain, not to one building. **It is the only role that is
not on any restaurant's payroll.** That is why it did not appear among the basic roles in
Part Two.

It sits above every restaurant and holds the head chefs. You can then spend your attention
on direction instead of supervising each kitchen day by day.

This only works if the area manager runs without you in the loop. It must not need you to
watch its work, settle its daily questions or remind it to read the week's records. **A
layer that still needs your attention has saved no attention.**

## One weekly report replaces many incidents

The area manager reads the operations log of every restaurant, across all of them at once.
Each log stays with its own restaurant. The shared view comes from reading them together,
not from moving them into one record.

Reading matters. A count can say how many complaints arrived, but not whether they point to
the same weakness. **The area manager brings you the pattern, not a pile of incidents.**

Once a week, its report shows:

- where dishes are coming out wrong in more than one restaurant;
- where the same complaint keeps arriving; and
- where a step is costing time it does not need to cost.

The report asks the same two questions every week: **is the kitchen getting more efficient,
and is the dish getting better?** Everything included must help answer one of them.

## The view across restaurants changes what you can know

A head chef can see a bad night in one kitchen. Only the area manager can see whether the
same thing is happening elsewhere. **That cross-restaurant view separates one bad night
from a fault in the manual.** It tells you whether the decision concerns one restaurant or
the whole chain.

The area manager reports that distinction. It does not change the manual or step into a
kitchen to correct the work itself.

## The report ends with decisions, never owner tasks

The weekly report ends with your decision list. Each item names a choice that needs your
direction. It never tells you to inspect a kitchen, chase an answer or perform work that
belongs below you.

**What reaches you is a decision, never a task.** The area manager gathers the evidence,
finds the pattern and frames the choice before the report arrives. That is how it keeps you
out of the daily loop while leaving direction in your hands.

## Responsibility still ends with the owner

Every chain of checks must stop somewhere. Adding another role to check the last role would
only begin the same question again. **The chain stops with you.**

The area manager holds the chains of checks once there are more restaurants than you can
personally hold. It does not become another checker. It does not taste dishes, and it does
not judge the doctor. Those limits matter because carrying reports upward is not the same
as giving a verdict.

You receive the choices and remain responsible for them. The area manager protects your
attention; it does not take your place.

## Never turn the area manager into another checker

Seeing every log can make checking every dish look like the natural next step. Do not allow
it. **The moment the area manager starts tasting dishes or judging other checks, it loses
the view it exists to provide.** Its job is to find patterns across restaurants and bring
you decisions, not to add one more verdict inside a kitchen.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Treat the area manager as a chain-level role above every restaurant. Never place it on a
   restaurant's payroll or among the basic restaurant roles.
2. Have the area manager hold the head chefs and run without the owner in the daily loop.
3. Read every restaurant's operations log across the chain while keeping each log with its
   own restaurant.
4. Bring the owner one report a week showing patterns, not a pile of incidents or bare
   counts.
5. Report repeated wrong dishes, repeated complaints and steps costing needless time.
6. Ask two questions in every report: is the kitchen getting more efficient, and is the
   dish getting better?
7. Use the view across restaurants to distinguish one bad night from a fault in the manual.
8. End every report with decisions for the owner to make. Never give the owner tasks to
   perform.
9. End responsibility at the owner. Have the area manager hold the chains of checks, but
   never let it taste dishes, give checking verdicts or judge the doctor.
