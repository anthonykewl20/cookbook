# 19. Checking it before it leaves the kitchen

**What this chapter settles:** how a finished dish leaves its maker, receives two independent verdicts, and waits after approval.

## The dish leaves the maker's hands before judgment

When cooking ends, the cook places the dish on the pass counter. **Neither checker goes to the cook's separate bench.** The dish must leave the maker's hands before it is judged.

The head chef and taster examine it there. It waits on the counter between their verdicts. Neither checker made it. The cook's taste note is evidence only, never approval.

## Two checks answer different questions

**The head chef answers whether the method was followed. The taster answers whether the quality is right.** One answer cannot supply the other.

The doctor's question — whether everything is still fit — concerns the means and belongs to chapter 26.

## The head chef compares promise with fact

The head chef reads what the instructions said should happen and the operations log's truthful account of what the cook did.

**The head chef compares the two on this dish.** If they agree, the verdict is *followed*. If they disagree, the verdict is *not followed*, with the difference stated. This is the drift check made live: written promise against recorded fact.

## The taster questions the plate itself

The taster follows this written checklist on every dish:

- Is this the dish that was ordered?
- Is every part promised by the recipe present?
- Does its appearance match the finished dish the recipe promises?
- Do its smell, taste and texture match that promise?
- Is that quality true throughout the dish, not only in the first part checked?
- Is anything wrong that the customer's palate would otherwise discover?
- From the plate itself, is the quality *good* or *bad*?

**These questions travel unchanged to every restaurant.** They are an instruction, not a taster's memory or preference, so the same plate faces the same test whoever is working.

The taster weighs the head chef's verdict, but still answers every question about the plate. **The taster may never reason, “The process was followed, so the plate must be fine.”** Its own quality verdict is final.

## The two verdicts reveal four outcomes

| Method | Quality | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Followed | Good | Send it. |
| Followed | Bad | The recipe is wrong. This is the manual's fault, not the cook's. |
| Not followed | Bad | The cook drifted. Fix the cook, not the recipe. |
| Not followed | Good | This is a fluke, and the most dangerous outcome. It cannot be repeated. Never bank it as success. |

A followed but bad result shows the recipe needs changing. This chapter records that finding; it does not change the manual.

Both verdicts are stamped into the operations log under the roles that gave them, never personal names. They are added as what happened; the earlier cook's note remains separate evidence.

## Send it means leave it ready on the pass counter

Only a followed, good dish is approved. **“Send it” means the approved dish stays on the pass counter until the waiter collects it.** The verdict gives permission, and the counter gives the dish a known place to wait. This chapter ends at that collection; serving belongs to chapter 20.

## Missing checkers stop the kitchen

If the head chef or taster cannot be covered without letting a maker approve their own work, the check cannot be made. **No checker, no service.** The kitchen closes until both checks can be made.

## Never turn obedience into quality

The dangerous shortcut is to say, "The process was followed, so the plate must be fine." It feels efficient because the first verdict already exists.

Never do it. **A followed recipe can still produce a bad dish.** The taster gives a quality verdict on every dish from the checklist and the plate itself.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Have the cook place every finished dish on the pass counter. Keep both checkers away from the cook's separate bench, and leave the dish on the counter between verdicts.
2. Never let either checker make the dish being checked. Never let the cook's taste note count as approval.
3. Give every dish the head chef's verdict on whether the method was followed and the taster's final verdict on whether the quality is right. Leave the doctor's question about whether everything is still fit to chapter 26.
4. Have the head chef compare the recipe and other written instructions with the operations log for that dish. Give *followed* when they agree; otherwise give *not followed* and state the difference.
5. Have the taster ask every written checklist question of every plate and give *good* or *bad* from the plate itself.
6. Carry the taster's checklist unchanged to every restaurant. Never replace it with memory or preference.
7. Let the taster weigh the head chef's verdict. Never use it in place of any checklist answer or the taster's own verdict.
8. Approve only a followed, good dish. Treat followed and bad as a wrong recipe that the manual needs to change; not followed and bad as cook drift; and not followed but good as a fluke that may never be banked as success. Record the finding here without changing the manual.
9. Stamp both verdicts into the operations log under roles, never personal names. Keep the cook's taste note as evidence, separate from the taster's verdict.
10. Leave an approved dish on the pass counter until the waiter collects it.
11. Apply “No checker, no service” when either check cannot be made without a maker approving their own work. Close the kitchen until both checks can be made.
12. Require a taster's quality verdict on every dish. Never conclude that following the process proves the plate is good.
