# 19. Checking it before it leaves the kitchen

**What this chapter settles:** how every finished dish receives a check of its method and a separate, final check of its quality before it goes any farther.

## Two checks answer different questions

A finished dish needs two verdicts. **The head chef answers whether the method was followed. The taster answers whether the quality is right.** One answer cannot supply the other.

The doctor's question — whether everything is still fit — belongs to chapter 26. It is about the means, not either verdict here.

Neither checker made the dish. The cook's own taste note is evidence, not approval. The head chef and taster act as roles, never as named people, and the taster never cooked the dish it judges.

## The head chef compares promise with fact

The head chef reads what the recipe and other instructions said should happen. Then the head chef reads the truthful account of what the cook actually did in the operations log.

**The head chef compares the two on this dish.** If they agree, the verdict is *followed*. If they disagree, the verdict is *not followed*, with the difference stated. This is the drift check made live: written promise against recorded fact.

## The taster questions the plate itself

The taster follows this written checklist on every dish:

- Is this the dish that was ordered?
- Is every part promised by the recipe present?
- Does its appearance match the finished dish the recipe promises?
- Do its smell, taste and texture match that promise?
- Is that quality true throughout the dish, not only in the first part checked?
- Is anything wrong that the customer's palate would otherwise discover?
- From the plate itself, is the quality *good* or *bad*?

**These questions travel unchanged to every restaurant.** They are an instruction, not a taster's memory or preference, so the same plate faces the same test whoever is working.

The taster weighs the head chef's verdict as evidence, but still answers every question about the plate. The taster's quality verdict is final.

## The two verdicts reveal four outcomes

| Method | Quality | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| Followed | Good | Send it. |
| Followed | Bad | The recipe is wrong. This is the manual's fault, not the cook's. |
| Not followed | Bad | The cook drifted. Fix the cook, not the recipe. |
| Not followed | Good | This is a fluke, and the most dangerous outcome. It cannot be repeated. Never bank it as success. |

A followed but bad result is how the book learns that its recipe needs changing. This chapter records that finding; it does not change the manual.

Both verdicts are stamped into the operations log under the roles that gave them, never personal names. They are added as what happened; the earlier cook's note remains evidence and is not turned into the taster's verdict.

## Missing checkers stop the kitchen

If the head chef or taster cannot be covered without letting a maker approve their own work, the check cannot be made. **No checker, no service.** The kitchen closes until both checks can be made.

## Never turn obedience into quality

The dangerous shortcut is to say, "The process was followed, so the plate must be fine." It feels efficient because the first verdict already exists.

Never do it. **A followed recipe can still produce a bad dish.** The taster gives a quality verdict on every dish from the checklist and the plate itself.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Give every finished dish two verdicts before it goes farther: the head chef's verdict on whether the method was followed and the taster's final verdict on whether the quality is right. Leave the doctor's question about whether everything is still fit to chapter 26.
2. Never let either checker make the dish being checked. Never let the cook's taste note count as approval.
3. Have the head chef compare the recipe and other written instructions with the operations log for that dish. Give *followed* when they agree; otherwise give *not followed* and state the difference.
4. Have the taster ask every written checklist question of every plate and give *good* or *bad* from the plate itself.
5. Carry the taster's checklist unchanged to every restaurant. Never replace it with memory or preference.
6. Let the taster weigh the head chef's verdict only as evidence. Never use it in place of any checklist answer or the taster's own verdict.
7. Send a followed, good dish onward. Treat followed and bad as a wrong recipe that the manual needs to change; not followed and bad as cook drift; and not followed but good as a fluke that may never be banked as success. Record the finding here without changing the manual.
8. Stamp both verdicts into the operations log under roles, never personal names. Keep the cook's taste note as evidence, separate from the taster's verdict.
9. Apply “No checker, no service” when either check cannot be made without a maker approving their own work. Close the kitchen until both checks can be made.
10. Require a taster's quality verdict on every dish. Never conclude that following the process proves the plate is good.
