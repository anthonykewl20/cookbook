# 18. Cooking it

**What this chapter settles:** how the cook follows the written recipe from the first cooking step to the finished dish, then records what happened without approving it.

## One cook makes one dish

The written order is in the kitchen, and the ingredients are ready. **Now the cook takes one dish and makes it.** The cook is the role from chapter 9, not a personal name.

At the smallest restaurant, the same cook also did the prep work in chapter 17. That work still came first and had its own written steps. Cooking begins after it and is the active making of the dish.

The doorman from chapter 13 allows the cook to work only at a separate bench. **The dish is made there, never in the shared main kitchen**, because separation keeps one chef's work from being mixed with shared work before it is admitted.

## The written recipe leads every step

The recipe is the instruction from chapter 10. It tells the cook what should happen. Equipment from chapter 5 and utensils from chapter 6 are already in the kitchen. This chapter does not sort them again. The cook uses the things the recipe names, in the way it states.

Follow these steps for one dish.

1. **Take responsibility for the dish as the cook for this shift.** Keep the role and shift ready for the log; do not replace them with a personal name.
2. **Work at the separate bench allowed by the doorman.** Do not move the work into the shared main kitchen for convenience.
3. **Read the written recipe from the first cooking step.** Do not cook from memory, because memory leaves no written promise against which the work can later be compared.
4. **Perform each cooking step in the written order.** Use only the equipment and utensils it calls for, exactly as it directs. Do not skip, add, exchange or improve a step.
5. **Continue to the end of that recipe for that one dish.** Do not turn away to another dish halfway through and leave either account unclear.
6. **After the cooking has happened, add the third stamp to the operations log.** Record the cook by role and shift, what was cooked, how it was cooked and how it tasted.

The recipe says what should happen. The stamp says what did happen. **Write what the cook actually did, even if it differs from the recipe**, because chapter 12's log is a diary. It is only added to at the end and never rewritten to hide a difference.

## A taste note is not approval

The cook records how the dish tasted as evidence of what happened. That note is not a checking verdict and does not mean the dish may leave the kitchen.

**The maker never approves the dish.** The two checks come next, in chapter 19, from hands that did not make it. This chapter ends with cooking and its truthful stamp.

## Four mistakes show the cooking has drifted

Check these mistakes in the order the work happens.

1. **The cook works in the shared main kitchen.** The separate-bench protection has been bypassed.
2. **The cook works from memory.** There is no steady instruction leading the dish.
3. **The cook changes a step.** Even a better result would be a different promise in one restaurant.
4. **The stamp is early, incomplete or flattering.** Then the log does not say what actually happened.

## Never improve one dish locally

It is tempting to adjust a step when the cook can see a better way. Do not. **A private improvement is drift, even when the dish tastes better.** Keep following the written recipe. Any change to the manual must pass through the one door and be made by the owner once for everybody.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Have the cook take one dish after its written prep steps are complete, and cook it from the first cooking step to the end of the recipe.
2. Identify the cook by role and shift, never by personal name.
3. Have the doorman allow the cook to work only at a separate bench. Never cook in the shared main kitchen.
4. Use the written recipe throughout. Never cook from memory.
5. Perform every cooking step in its written order, using only the equipment and utensils it names and exactly as it directs. Never skip, add, exchange or improve a step.
6. Add the third stamp only after cooking has happened. Record who took the dish by role and shift, what they cooked, how they cooked it and how it tasted.
7. Record what actually happened. Add only to the end of the operations log, and never rewrite it to hide a difference from the recipe.
8. Treat the cook's taste note as evidence, never as approval or a checking verdict.
9. Never let the cook approve the dish. Leave the two checks to hands that did not make it.
10. Keep the written recipe unchanged during the work. Send any possible change through the one door for the owner to decide and make once for everybody.
