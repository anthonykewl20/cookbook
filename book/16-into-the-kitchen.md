# 16. Into the kitchen

**What this chapter settles:** how a written order moves from the front of the restaurant to the kitchen without changing or disappearing.

## The written order crosses unchanged

The front and back need one clear hand-off. **The waiter carries the written order, and the kitchen receives that same written order.** Nothing is retold from memory. Nothing is copied into different words.

The kitchen already exists as part of the restaurant, as chapter 3 settled; this chapter deals only with the order crossing into it. The waiter stays the waiter. Carrying the order to the kitchen does not give the waiter any kitchen work.

## The kitchen receives before anyone begins

An order has arrived only when the head chef or a cook receives it. Leaving it near the kitchen is not a hand-off, because nobody on the kitchen side has received it.

The choice of receiver keeps the roles clear. **The head chef runs the kitchen and manages the chefs, while a cook belongs to the kitchen work.** The waiter hands over the order and stops there, in keeping with the boundaries set in chapter 9.

Receiving the order is not the same as a chef taking responsibility for the dish. Arrival is only the second stamp. Which chef takes it belongs to the next stamp, so no chef details are added yet.

## Three actions complete the hand-off

Do these in order.

1. **The waiter carries the same written order from the front to the kitchen.**
2. **The head chef or a cook receives it without rewriting it.**
3. **Add the second stamp to the order's log: “It reached the kitchen.”**

The arrival is added when the hand-off happens. This matters because the log is the restaurant's memory, as chapter 12 explains. An unstamped order can disappear between front and back while the record still looks complete.

## Three failures expose a broken hand-off

Check these failures in order.

1. **There is no second stamp.** Do not assume the order arrived. The log does not show the hand-off.
2. **The kitchen holds different words.** The order was rewritten while moving, so the kitchen did not receive the order the waiter carried.
3. **The second stamp names the chef who took the dish.** Arrival has been confused with the next stamp. Keep “It reached the kitchen” on its own.

Also stop if the waiter begins kitchen work. That blurs the roles and turns a hand-off into one person crossing both sides.

## Never rewrite an order while handing it over

It can be tempting to make the order neater for the kitchen. Do not. **A helpful rewrite creates another telling of the order, and the telephone game begins.** The hand-off exists to move the written words unchanged, not to improve them on the way.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Carry the same written order from the front of the restaurant to the kitchen.
2. Hand the written order to the head chef or a cook. Never leave it for the kitchen to find.
3. Keep the waiter outside kitchen work. End the waiter's part when the order is handed over.
4. Never rewrite, retell or copy the order during the hand-off.
5. Add the second stamp when the kitchen receives the order: “It reached the kitchen.”
6. Never claim that an unstamped order reached the kitchen.
7. Keep arrival separate from the next stamp. Never name which chef took the dish in the arrival stamp.
