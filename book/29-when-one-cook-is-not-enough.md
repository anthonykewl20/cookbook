# 29. When one cook is not enough — and what the head chef's job turns into

**What this chapter settles:** when to add more cooks, what must be written first, and how the head chef coordinates them while keeping the method verdict on every dish.

## Attention runs out before the orders do

A long line of orders is not enough reason to add a cook. A line can shrink. **The strain that matters is that one cook can no longer hold the whole cooking job in view.** Their attention is spent moving among prep, cooking and the recipe's instructions. Something then gets missed even when the work is familiar.

That is the growth signal. You are protecting the cook's attention, not trying to make every busy moment disappear.

## The whole job is written before another cook arrives

An extra pair of hands cannot inherit memory. **Nothing is handed over until a stranger could perform the whole job from what is written.**

The written prep and cooking steps must lead that stranger through the dish in order. The recipe must say where prep ends, how cooking proceeds and what each instruction requires. If the first cook still has to explain a gap, the job is not ready to grow.

Written instruction comes before the extra pair of hands, never after.

## More hands still wear the cook hat

Adding cooks does not invent another role. Each new pair of hands wears the same cook hat and follows the same written job. **Name each cook by role and shift, never by person.**

The log's stamp records which role and shift cooked, what they cooked and how they cooked it. A cook's note about taste remains evidence only. It is never approval.

## The head chef gains coordination and keeps checking

Many cooks create another strain because the head chef's attention runs out while holding all their work in view together. **The head chef's job gains coordinating many cooks. It does not give up the method verdict on any dish.**

The method check is a reading job, not a watching job. The head chef reads what the instructions said should happen. The head chef then reads the log's truthful account of what the cook did and compares the two on that dish. The check never required standing over a bench and watching somebody work. Watching every cook cannot grow with the kitchen. Reading each dish's written account can.

Every dish still gets two verdicts from two different heads. The head chef decides whether the method was followed. The taster decides whether the quality is right and never cooks that dish. This remains true with one cook or fifty. The doctor still checks whether the people and building are fit. **No checker, no service.**

## Every other missing instruction still goes through one door

A missing coordination instruction is a fault in the writing. **Raise the fault from the kitchen, write it into the log and report it upward.** Let the owner amend the manual once for everybody. Never repair the recipe locally.

This gives the owner a decision, never the work of coordinating cooks. Every kitchen then receives the same settled instruction instead of passing local repairs along like a telephone game.

## Existing protections keep the cooks apart

The doorman keeps cooks at separate benches. The log identifies each cook by role and shift. These protections keep work apart and make each hand visible.

They do not settle every way two cooks might collide. Chapter 30 gives those detailed rules. This chapter only brings the extra cooks into the kitchen without weakening the checks.

## Never turn the head chef into the spare cook

When orders are waiting, it is tempting to put the head chef at a bench. Do not. **A head chef making dishes loses sight of the cooks and becomes a maker inside the work being coordinated.** The extra hands belong under the cook hat. The head chef coordinates them and keeps the method check.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Add more cooks only when one cook's attention can no longer hold prep, cooking and the recipe's instructions in view. Never use a pile of orders alone as the signal.
2. Add another cook only after the whole cook job is written well enough for a stranger to perform, including every prep and cooking step in order.
3. Add each extra pair of hands as another wearer of the cook hat. Identify every cook by role and shift, never by person.
4. Stamp what each cook did in the log. Treat a cook's taste note as evidence, never approval.
5. Make the head chef coordinate every cook while keeping the method verdict on every dish. Compare the written instructions with the log's account of that dish.
6. Keep the checks separate at every kitchen size. The head chef checks method, the taster checks quality without cooking that dish, and the doctor checks whether the people and building are fit. If any required checker is absent, do not serve. No checker, no service.
7. Raise any missing coordination instruction as a fault. Write it into the log and report it upward. Let the owner amend the manual once for everybody; never give the owner a coordination task or repair the recipe locally.
8. Keep cooks at the doorman's separate benches and use the log's role-and-shift stamps. Leave detailed collision rules to chapter 30.
9. Never turn the head chef into a spare cook. Put every extra pair of making hands under the cook hat.
