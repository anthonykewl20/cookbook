# 29. When one cook is not enough

**What this chapter settles:** when to add more cooks, what must be written first, and how the head chef's job changes without breaking the kitchen's checks.

## Attention runs out before the orders do

A long line of orders is not enough reason to add a cook. A line can shrink. **The strain that matters is that one cook can no longer hold the whole cooking job in view.** Their attention is spent moving among prep, cooking and the recipe's instructions, so something is missed even when the work is familiar.

That is the growth signal. You are protecting the cook's attention, not trying to make every busy moment disappear.

## The whole job is written before another cook arrives

An extra pair of hands cannot inherit memory. **Nothing is handed over until a stranger could perform the whole job from what is written.**

The prep steps written in chapter 17 and the cooking steps written in chapter 18 must lead that stranger through the dish in order. The recipe must say where prep ends, how cooking proceeds and what each instruction requires. If the first cook still has to explain a gap, the job is not ready to grow.

Written instruction comes before the extra pair of hands, never after.

## More hands still wear the cook hat

Adding cooks does not invent another role. Each new pair of hands wears the same cook hat and follows the same written job. **Name each cook by role and shift, never by person.**

The log's stamp records which role and shift cooked, what they cooked and how they cooked it. A cook's note about taste remains evidence only. It is never approval.

## The head chef coordinates without becoming a maker

Many cooks create a second strain. The head chef cannot check each dish and still hold all the cooks in view. **The head chef's job turns from checking each dish to coordinating many cooks.**

That change does not loosen the kitchen's separations. A cook makes and never approves. The taster still gives the final quality verdict. The doctor still checks whether the people and building are fit, and never treats what it finds. The head chef coordinates and does not step in to make dishes.

The existing method check cannot simply vanish. Every dish still requires a verdict on whether the written method was followed. This book has not yet settled who gives that verdict once the head chef stops checking each dish. Do not drop it and do not guess a replacement.

## The unanswered question goes through the one door

An unsettled rule is a fault in the writing. **Add the unanswered method-verdict question to the end of the log, report it upward and let the owner decide.** This gives the owner a decision, never the task of coordinating cooks. The owner amends the manual once for everybody.

Use the same door for any other missing coordination instruction. Raise the fault from the kitchen, write it into the log and report it upward. Do not repair the recipe locally.

**Do not put a second cook on until the owner has settled who gives the method verdict.** Growing past one cook before then would leave every dish without one of its two required verdicts. No checker, no service: if the check cannot be made, the restaurant does not serve.

## Existing protections help without settling every collision

The doorman already keeps cooks at separate benches. The log already identifies their role and shift. These protections help keep work apart and make each hand visible.

They do not settle every way two cooks might collide. Chapter 30 gives those detailed rules. This chapter only gets the extra cooks and their coordinator into the kitchen without weakening the checks.

## Never turn the head chef into the spare cook

When orders are waiting, it is tempting to put the head chef at a bench. Do not. **A head chef making dishes loses sight of the cooks and becomes a maker inside the work being coordinated.** The extra hands belong under the cook hat. The head chef keeps attention on the kitchen.

## The rules, flat

obeys.*
