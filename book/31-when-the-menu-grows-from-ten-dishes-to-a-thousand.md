# 31. When the menu grows from ten dishes to a thousand

**What this chapter settles:** why a much larger menu loads the same restaurant without changing its rules, and what must be true before the restaurant grows with it.

## A larger menu loads the same kitchen

With ten dishes, a waiter may hold the menu and the open orders in view at once. With a
thousand, one may slip from attention while the other is being handled. The cashier must
recognise many more dishes when taking the money. The cook must find and follow the right
recipe without confusing it with another. The pantry must be ready for the ingredients
called for by many more possible orders.

**The menu has grown, but the kind of work has not changed.** The waiter still takes the
order. The cashier still takes the money. The cook still follows a recipe. The pantry still
holds stock. The base framework does not break under the larger load. It grows by the rules
already given.

## Every offered dish already has a recipe

A restaurant keeps its own menu. It may offer any number of the chain's dishes, up to the
full set. **It may offer fewer dishes than the chain, but never different ones.**

The menu is local. The recipe is not. Every dish added to the local menu must already have a
written recipe in the chain's manual. That recipe travels unchanged to the restaurant. A
larger local menu selects more of those fixed dishes; it does not create local versions of
them.

This must be true before menu size leads to another pair of hands. A stranger must be able
to make every offered dish from its writing. Otherwise, handing over the work changes the
dish according to who happens to be standing there. **Written instruction comes before the
extra pair of hands, never after.**

## Attention decides when the restaurant grows

A thousand dishes do not by themselves order a growth step. Neither does a pile of waiting
work. **Someone's attention must be running out.**

Watch the role carrying the strain. Can the waiter still hold the menu and open orders in
view? Can the cashier still take each payment correctly while many kinds of dishes are being
served? Can the cook still reach the right recipe and keep the order in view? Growth begins
when the answer becomes no, because that is when part of the job is disappearing from notice.

The number of dishes tells you where to look. Attention tells you whether to act. If extra
hands are needed, the earlier growth rules govern them; the larger menu does not suspend
those rules.

## The building already contains what the menu needs

Part Two has already placed each part in the building. Chapter 10 names the menu and recipes
as instructions. Chapter 7 names the ingredients as stock. Chapter 9 names the people by
role. **A larger menu adds load to these categories; it does not invent a new category.**

The pantry's work may become heavier because more recipes call for more kinds of stock.
Chapter 32 handles how the pantry itself grows. Here, the point is only that it remains the
pantry doing stock work.

## Three checks stay the same at every size

At ten dishes and at a thousand, the head chef checks the method, the taster checks the
quality and the doctor checks whether the people and building remain fit. **Menu size never
combines, weakens or replaces these checks.**

The seven base roles still hold. So do the three separate pairs of hands: maker, checker and
doctor. A larger choice of dishes does not make self-approval safe.

## Never write a local recipe for a larger menu

It is tempting to treat a newly offered dish as new local work and adjust its recipe to suit
the restaurant. Do not. The restaurant controls which chain dishes appear on its menu, not
what those dishes become. A local recipe would make the same name promise a different dish.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Keep the waiter, cashier, cook and pantry inside their existing work as the menu grows.
2. Keep each restaurant's menu local. Allow it to offer fewer chain dishes, but never a
   different dish.
3. Add a dish to a local menu only when its written recipe already exists in the chain's
   manual and travels unchanged to the restaurant.
4. Put written recipes that a stranger can follow before any extra pair of hands needed
   because of menu size.
5. Take a growth step only when someone's attention runs out. Never use the number of dishes
   or a pile of work alone as the signal.
6. Treat menus and recipes as instructions, ingredients as stock and people as roles.
7. Leave the pantry's own growth to chapter 32.
8. Keep the head chef's method check, the taster's quality check and the doctor's fitness
   check unchanged at every menu size.
9. Keep the seven base roles and the separate maker, checker and doctor at every menu size.
10. Never write or use a local recipe for a chain dish.
