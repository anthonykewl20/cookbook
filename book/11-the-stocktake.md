# 11. The stocktake

**What this chapter settles:** how to keep one reliable answer to “what have we got?” without confusing it with what happened before.

## The stocktake says what the building has now

Every restaurant needs a standing list of what its building has right now. **That list is the stocktake, and it answers one question: “what have we got?”**

“Right now” does the work. The stocktake does not tell you what arrived yesterday or what left this morning. It tells you what is present when you look. A new cook can read it and begin from the same answer you would give.

The stocktake is a record. Do not confuse it with the pantry ingredients that run out, which were settled in chapter 7. Those ingredients may appear in the count, but they are not the stocktake. The stocktake is the answer written down.

## Four counts describe the whole building

The stocktake covers four things:

1. **What is in the pantry.**
2. **What is in the safe.**
3. **Which benches exist.**
4. **Who is on the payroll, as a count of people.**

Together, these tell a cook what the restaurant has to work with. The payroll entry is only a number here. It does not say what each person does or how anyone is hired.

## A changed answer replaces the old answer

**Rewrite the stocktake every time the answer changes.** If the pantry count changes, the standing list changes. If a bench is added, the standing list changes. If the payroll count changes, the standing list changes.

A stocktake is a photograph. When you take a new one, you throw away the old one. Keeping both as if both described the building now would give the next cook two answers to one question.

That makes the stocktake useful even when the cooks change. Nobody has to remember the last shift. They only need the one standing list that is true now.

## The photograph and the diary pass different tests

There is a simple test for deciding where a record belongs. **If it answers “what have we got now?”, it belongs in the stocktake. If it tells what happened in order, it belongs in the operations log.**

The stocktake is a photograph: replace it when the answer changes. The operations log is a diary: add only to its end. That is enough to separate them here; chapter 12 settles the diary itself.

## Two kinds of record must never become one thing

It is tempting to keep the stocktake and the operations log in one place. Both concern the same restaurant, so one combined record can look tidy.

Do not combine them. **Replacing the photograph would throw away the diary, while keeping the diary would leave old photographs beside the current one.** One place cannot obey both rules. The stocktake and the operations log are two kinds of record, and they must not be one thing.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Keep one standing stocktake that answers “what have we got right now?”
2. Record what is in the pantry, what is in the safe, which benches exist and the number of people on the payroll.
3. Rewrite the stocktake every time any answer changes, and discard the old stocktake.
4. Treat the stocktake as a record of the current answer, never as the category of ingredients that run out.
5. Put a record in the stocktake when it describes what the building has now. Put it in the operations log when it tells what happened in order.
6. Keep the stocktake and the operations log separate. Never combine the photograph with the diary.
