# 24. Reading the log back

**What this chapter settles:** how to read the operations log and see what the restaurant actually did, where it lost time, where it went wrong and what must be remembered.

## Read what happened, not what should have happened

Chapter 12 put the operations log in the restaurant. Now you read it back. **The log is the
account of what actually happened, in the order it happened.** The instructions are a
different thing. They say what should have happened.

Begin with the completed log for this restaurant. Read its entries in order, from the start
of the day to the closing entry. Do not begin with an explanation from a chef. The chef may
remember only part of the day, while the diary keeps the whole account after the shift has
ended.

## The order of events reveals the procedure

Read one order from its first entry to its last. Then read the next. **The path the work took
is the procedure actually followed.** It may match the written instructions. It may include
a wait, a return or a repeated piece of work. Read what is there without smoothing those
turns away.

This matters because a familiar result can hide an unfamiliar route. A dish may have
reached the customer even though the kitchen took steps in the wrong order. Only the diary
shows the route that produced it.

## Lost time appears where work stops or repeats

Read again for places where an order waited, went back or had work done twice. **Those
entries show where time was wasted because they show work that did not move the order
forward.** A single delay may belong to one difficult day. The same delay appearing again
shows where the restaurant keeps losing time.

Do not turn this reading into a guess about who is slow. The log can show where time went.
It cannot replace the separate check of whether people and the building are still fit.

## Errors remain visible in the diary

Next, read for the moments when the intended course broke: an event happened wrongly, had
to be repeated or sent the order back. **The log shows where things went wrong because it
keeps the failed course as well as what happened afterwards.**

Do not treat a later success as proof that the earlier error no longer matters. The second
attempt may have produced the dish, but the first attempt still explains the waste and the
risk of the same thing happening again.

## The diary remembers after every chef leaves

Every chef is a stranger who leaves at the end of the shift. The next chef cannot remember
the day, and the instructions cannot tell them what happened. **The log is the restaurant's
only memory of its own actions.**

That is why the four readings belong together. The same diary preserves the procedure, the
waste, the errors and the memory. This chapter reads one restaurant's record. The area
manager's report across restaurants is separate work.

## Disagreement makes drift easiest to see

Finally, place the instructions beside the log. Compare what should have happened with what
did happen. **Where they disagree, you have found drift. This is the sharpest place to see
it, not the only place.**

Stop here at seeing what the records show. Saying something is wrong belongs to chapter 25.
Reading the evidence and raising a fault are separate acts.

## Never tidy the diary while reading it

It is tempting to rewrite an awkward entry once you know how the day ended. Do not. **The
disagreement, delay or error is the reason you are reading.** If you make the diary agree
with the instructions, you destroy the evidence.

The operations log only grows at its end. Never erase, move, tidy or rewrite an earlier
entry.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Read each restaurant's completed operations log in the order its events happened.
2. Read each order from its first entry to its last to identify the procedure actually
   followed.
3. Identify wasted time where work waited, went back or was repeated.
4. Identify where things went wrong without hiding an error behind a later success.
5. Treat the operations log as the restaurant's only memory of its actions after the chefs
   leave.
6. Compare the instructions with the log. Treat every disagreement as drift, and treat this
   comparison as the sharpest place to see drift, never the only place.
7. Stop at reading what the records show. Leave raising a fault to chapter 25.
8. Never erase, move, tidy or rewrite an earlier log entry. Add only at the end.
