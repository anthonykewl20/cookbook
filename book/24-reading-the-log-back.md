# 24. Reading the log back

**What this chapter settles:** who reads the operations log, what each role looks for, and how the true day exposes waste, errors and drift.

## The roster gives each question a reader

Reading the log back is not a job for unnamed eyes. **The head chef reads whether the
process was followed. The taster reads what the log records about whether the quality was
right. The doctor reads whether the people and the building are still fit.** These are the
three questions given to the roster in chapter 9.

The questions stay separate. A head chef may find an error in the method, but cannot turn
that into a verdict on quality. A taster may find a bad result, but cannot decide from that
alone that the method was not followed. The doctor's health check belongs to chapter 26.

## The diary shows the day that happened

Part Two put the operations log in the building in chapter 12. It records what actually
happened, in the order it happened. **Read that order from beginning to end before comparing
it with what should have happened.** Otherwise the instructions can tempt you to remember
the day as intended rather than as lived.

The head chef follows the work through the entries. This reveals the procedure the kitchen
actually followed: what happened first, what waited, what was repeated and what was missed.
It also reveals wasted time. Waiting, repeating work and a step that keeps taking longer
are visible because the diary keeps their place in the day.

## Different waste belongs to different eyes

Reading for procedure and reading for waste are not always the same pair of eyes. **The
head chef reads delay in the running of the kitchen. The doctor reads repeated slowing only
when it may show that a person or the building is no longer fit.** Chapter 26 settles that
health question; this chapter only makes sure the sign is not overlooked.

Errors divide in the same way. The head chef reads errors in the method. The taster reads
the recorded results and quality verdicts. The doctor keeps to signs about the people and
the building. No role borrows another role's question merely because all three are reading
the same diary.

The log shows where things went wrong because it keeps missed steps, repeated work, bad
results and what came after them. A later success does not erase the earlier error.

## The sharpest comparison leaves disagreement visible

Now place the log beside the written instructions. The instructions say what should
happen. The log says what did happen. **Where they disagree is drift, and this comparison
is the sharpest place to see it.** It is not the only place drift can appear.

Do not repair the disagreement on the page. The disagreement is the finding. If reading
uncovers one, it goes at the end of the log and upward like any other fault. Chapter 25
handles that next step; finding a fault after the day does not make it less real than seeing
one happen.

## The log remembers after every shift leaves

Every chef is a stranger who leaves at the end of the shift. The next stranger cannot ask
the vanished shift what it did or why it waited. **The log is the restaurant's only
memory because it stays when the people do not.**

That memory is useful only while it remains truthful. It must preserve the procedure, the
waste and the errors exactly as they were recorded.

## Never improve the diary while reading it

It is tempting to tidy an entry when the true sequence looks confused or wrong. Do not.
Never erase, move or rewrite an earlier entry to make it agree with the instructions.
Add any correction or new finding at the end, because changing the past destroys the answer
you came to read.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Have the head chef read the log for the procedure followed and delay in running the kitchen.
2. Have the taster read the recorded results and quality verdicts.
3. Leave signs about the fitness of people and the building to the doctor and the health check in chapter 26.
4. Keep each role to its own question. Never let one role give another role's verdict.
5. Read the operations log from beginning to end before comparing it with the instructions.
6. Read for what happened, what waited, what was repeated, what was missed and what kept taking longer.
7. Compare the log with the written instructions. Treat every disagreement as drift and this comparison as the sharpest place to see it, never the only place.
8. Put every fault found while reading at the end of the log and report it upward as chapter 25 requires.
9. Treat the log as the restaurant's only memory of the procedure, waste and errors after each shift leaves.
10. Never erase, move, tidy or rewrite an earlier log entry. Add any correction or new finding at the end.
