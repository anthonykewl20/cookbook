# 37. Knowing which restaurant you are standing in

**What this chapter settles:** how to establish which restaurant's facts you are reading before you judge what has gone wrong.

## This is the most dangerous confusion in the book

A wrong recipe can spoil one dish. **A wrong restaurant can make every piece of true evidence tell you the wrong story.** Another restaurant's log may be accurate. Its stocktake may be current. You can still raise the wrong fault if you read either as a record of the place in front of you.

That is why you establish the restaurant before judging its work. A believable record from the wrong place is more dangerous than an obvious mistake, because nothing on the page warns you to stop.

## Instructions travel and records never do

The rule for the whole chain is simple: **Instructions travel. Records never do.**

Recipes, house rules, the taster's checklist and job descriptions are identical in every restaurant. This book is installed in every restaurant too. Those instructions tell you the chain's promise. They cannot tell you which restaurant you are standing in, because the same words appear everywhere.

Do not treat their sameness as proof that the records are shared. A record belongs only to the restaurant whose life it describes.

## The restaurant's own life identifies the place

Look at what never travels. **The building, staff, stocktake and operations log belong to this restaurant alone.**

The stocktake is a photograph of what this place has now. Match its building, stock and staff to the place around you. The operations log is the diary of what happened here. Its entries must describe this kitchen's own work, in order, and another restaurant's entries never belong in it.

Use both records. The stocktake answers, "Is this the place described here?" The log answers, "Is this the place where these events happened?" If either answer is no, stop. You are reading the wrong restaurant's life.

## The menu is local but the recipes are shared

The menu is the one local instruction. **It may vary by restaurant, but only by offering fewer chain dishes, never different ones.** It can therefore help confirm which restaurant you are in.

The menu does not change the chain's promise. A shorter menu says that this restaurant does not serve one of the dishes. It does not say that the dish or its recipe changed. Chapter 42 settles this local right in full.

## The wrong place creates the wrong fault

Check for these mistakes before reporting trouble.

1. **A local log entry is treated as chain-wide drift.** One restaurant's diary tells you what happened there. It does not prove that every restaurant's instructions moved.
2. **Another stocktake is treated as this restaurant's photograph.** You then report the wrong shortage, building or staff.
3. **A shorter local menu is treated as a recipe change.** The dish was removed here; its shared recipe was not rewritten.

## Never judge before you name the restaurant

It is tempting to begin with the recipes because they look official and complete. Do not. **Shared instructions identify the promise, not the place.** First match the local stocktake and log to the building and staff in front of you. Only then compare what should have happened with what did.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Establish which restaurant's records you are reading before judging its work.
2. Treat recipes, house rules, the taster's checklist, job descriptions and this book as travelling instructions that cannot identify one restaurant.
3. Keep every restaurant's building, staff, stocktake and operations log with that restaurant alone.
4. Match the stocktake and operations log to the building and staff in front of you. Stop if either belongs to another restaurant.
5. Treat the menu as the one local instruction. Allow fewer chain dishes, never different ones.
6. Treat a shorter local menu as a local removal, never as a change to the shared recipe.
7. Never treat one restaurant's log entry as proof that the chain's instructions changed.
8. Identify the restaurant from its local life, then compare its instructions with its records.
