# 10. The instructions

**What this chapter settles:** what counts as an instruction, what stays fixed across the chain, and how it may be changed.

## Instructions speak before the work begins

**An instruction is anything written that tells someone what to do, before they do it.** It tells the next stranger what the restaurant expects without relying on anybody who worked yesterday.

The instructions include:

- the menu;
- the recipes;
- the house rules on the wall;
- each person's written job description; and
- this book on the shelf.

These are different kinds of writing, but they make the same sort of promise. The menu says what may be ordered. The recipe says how the dish is made. The house rules say how the place is run. A job description says what the person holding that job must do. This book says what every restaurant in the chain must do.

## The manual is the one thing that holds still

Taken together, the instructions are the manual. **The manual is static: it does not change during the work or because a particular chef prefers another way.**

Your chefs are never the same twice, and none of them remembers yesterday. If they fill the gaps from memory or preference, the result becomes a telephone game. If the people vary and the result must not, then the manual is the only thing allowed to hold still.

Static does not mean that a bad instruction stays forever. It means the same instruction applies to everybody until the owner changes it for everybody.

## Every change passes through one door

The manual changes only through this order:

1. Something goes wrong or could be better.
2. It is written into the log.
3. The log is read and reported upward.
4. The owner decides.
5. The manual is amended once, for everybody.

**The door opens from below, but only the owner amends the manual.** Any chef may raise a fault. No chef, head chef or restaurant may repair its own copy of a recipe, even when the repair seems obvious. Otherwise one restaurant begins serving a different promise from the rest.

## The promise travels unchanged

When a new restaurant opens, it receives the same instructions. **Instructions are the promise of what should happen, not a record of what happened in one place.** They therefore travel unchanged from restaurant to restaurant.

The book is installed. It is not transcribed. If you want it to say something different, you change the book through the one door above. You do not make a second version for one kitchen.

## Instructions and the log reveal drift together

Chapters 10 and 12 are the pair the whole book turns on. **Instructions say what should happen. The log says what did.**

When the two disagree, that is drift. It is the only place drift can be seen at all. Without the instructions, there is no promise to compare against. Without the log, there is no account of what the kitchen actually did.

## The taster's checklist is an instruction

The taster does not judge a dish by mood or memory. **The taster follows a written checklist, so every dish faces the same questions whoever is tasting.** That checklist is an instruction too. Chapter 19 gives it in detail.

## Never let one restaurant fix its own recipe

The tempting mistake is to correct a recipe where the fault was found. The correction may even improve that night's dish.

Do not do it. A private improvement changes the promise for one restaurant and hides the disagreement from the owner. Raise the fault from below, then let the owner amend the manual once for everybody.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Treat anything written that tells someone what to do before they do it as an instruction.
2. Include the menu, the recipes, the house rules, each person's written job description and this book among the instructions.
3. Keep the manual static during the work. Apply the same instruction to everybody until the owner changes it for everybody.
4. Raise every fault or possible improvement into the log, report it upward, obtain the owner's decision and amend the manual once for everybody.
5. Never allow a chef, head chef or restaurant to amend its own copy of an instruction.
6. Carry the instructions unchanged into every new restaurant. Treat them as the promise of what should happen, never as a record of what happened in one place.
7. Install this book; never transcribe it or create a second version for one kitchen.
8. Compare what the instructions say should happen with what the log says did happen, and identify every disagreement as drift.
9. Require the taster to follow the written checklist given in chapter 19.
