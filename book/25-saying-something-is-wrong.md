# 25. Saying something is wrong

**What this chapter settles:** how anybody in a restaurant raises a fault without quietly changing the chain's instructions.

## Every employee can open the door from below

The owner is not standing in every kitchen. A cook sees the recipe fail in the cook's hands. A waiter hears what reaches the table. A dishwasher finds what keeps coming back wrong. **Every employee who sees something wrong, or sees how it could be better, may raise the fault.**

The head chef will raise the most. Running the place reveals patterns that one shift or one dish may hide. That does not make the head chef the only door. Other employees see real faults too, and waiting for the head chef can make those faults disappear with the end of a shift.

## Writing it in the log is raising it

Saying something aloud is not enough. A conversation ends, a memory leaves, and the chain is left with nothing it can carry upward. **Write the fault into the operations log described in Chapter 12 before reporting it upward. The log is where a fault goes.**

Write what you observed. Say what went wrong or what could be better. Do not hand the owner a job such as “find out what happened.” The people in the kitchen saw what happened, so their part is to put that fact into the log.

A warning spoken during service may help someone at that moment. It still does not count as raising the fault until it is written. **A fault seen but not recorded is a fault the chain never had a chance to fix.** Failing to record it is itself a failure of the way the restaurant is run.

## The fault travels upward after it is written

The door opens from the bottom because the knowledge begins with the people doing the work. Once the fault is in the log, report that written fault upward. This chapter stops there. The later decision belongs to the owner, not to the kitchen that found the fault.

The order matters:

1. Notice what went wrong or could be better.
2. Write the observed fact into the operations log.
3. Report the written fault upward.
4. Keep following the instructions that already travel through the chain.

## Nobody in the restaurant repairs the manual

A cook, head chef or whole restaurant may be certain that the repair is obvious. **None of them may amend a recipe, this book or any other instruction that travels.** A private repair gives one restaurant a different promise and hides the fault from the rest of the chain.

The already-settled choice to shorten a restaurant's menu remains the one local exception. That removal is logged. It does not permit any other local change.

## Never turn a fault into a private improvement

The tempting mistake is to correct the instruction where the problem appeared. It feels quicker, and the next dish may even be better.

Do not do it. Record the fault and send it upward. **The kitchen has the right to raise the fault, not the right to rewrite the instruction.** Otherwise the chain loses both the evidence of the problem and the one shared promise every restaurant is meant to follow.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Allow every employee to raise anything that went wrong or could be better; never reserve that right for the head chef.
2. Write every fault into the operations log before reporting it upward. Treat speaking without recording as not raised.
3. Record the observed fact. Never send the owner a task to discover what the kitchen already saw.
4. Report the written fault upward, then keep following the instructions already in force.
5. Never allow a cook, head chef or restaurant to amend any recipe or other instruction that travels.
6. Preserve the one settled local exception: a restaurant may shorten its menu, and that removal is logged. Never treat it as permission for another local change.
