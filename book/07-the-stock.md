# 7. The stock

**What this chapter settles:** how to tell what counts as stock, and what information belongs with it.

## Stock must pass both halves of one test

**Stock is anything that runs out in ordinary work and has to be reordered.** Both halves
matter.

First ask whether making dishes uses the thing up. Then ask whether the restaurant must
order more of it so the work can continue. If both answers are yes, it is stock.

An ingredient passes. Each dish leaves less of it, and more must be ordered. A knife does
not pass. It may be lost or broken, but making an ordinary dish does not use it up. Damage,
loss and age are not ordinary use.

## The other four categories fail the stock test

The same test keeps nearby categories apart.

- **The building is fixed to the ground.** You would leave it behind if the restaurant
  moved. Ordinary work does not use it up.
- **Furniture can be carried out and is not used up.** A chair may wear with age, but a dish
  does not leave less chair behind.
- **Equipment is powered and does work for you.** An oven works through service; it is not
  used up by each dish.
- **A utensil is picked up by hand to do the work, then put back.** A knife remains after the
  dish. An ingredient does not.

Being movable is not enough. Being replaceable is not enough. **Ordinary work must run the
thing down, and the restaurant must reorder it.**

## Each ingredient comes with who supplies it

The ingredients are the worked example of stock. Flour, oil and tomatoes run out as dishes
are made, so the restaurant must order more.

**The ingredient is the stock, and who supplies it is the fact that belongs with it.** The
flour and the answer to “Who supplies the flour?” stay together. Otherwise, the restaurant
knows what must be reordered but not where it comes from.

This does not make the supplier a member of staff or a role in the kitchen. The supplier is
simply who provides that ingredient. What the ingredient costs is not stock. That belongs
with money.

## Stock exists before anyone decides how to use it

**Stock belongs in the furnished building.** This part of the book says the ingredients
exist there and identifies who supplies each one. It does not say how a cook uses them.

Part Two says a thing exists. Part Three says how it is used. Keep that boundary here,
because deciding use now would mix furnishing the restaurant with running it.

## Stock is a category, not today’s quantities

Stock answers, “What kind of thing is this?” It does not answer, “How much do we have right
now?”

**The standing list of current quantities is the stocktake in chapter 11.** Stock remains
the category. Do not turn it into that list, because the category and the present answer
are two different things.

## Never call every replaceable thing stock

It is tempting to call a chair, knife or oven stock because the restaurant may someday need
another one. Do not. **Needing a replacement one day is not the same as being used up in
ordinary work.** Apply both halves of the test every time.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Classify a thing as stock only when ordinary work uses it up and the restaurant must
   reorder it.
2. Exclude damage, loss and age from the test for ordinary use.
3. Classify fixed things as building, carried things that are not used up as furniture,
   powered things that do work as equipment, and hand-held things that remain after the work
   as utensils.
4. Name who supplies each ingredient classified as stock.
5. Treat a supplier as the provider of an ingredient, never as a member of staff or a
   kitchen role.
6. Keep an ingredient’s cost with money, not stock.
7. State that stock exists in the furnished building, and leave how it is used to Part
   Three.
8. Keep the stock category separate from the standing list of current quantities in chapter
   11.
9. Never classify a thing as stock merely because it is movable, replaceable or may wear
   out.
