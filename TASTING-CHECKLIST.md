# The tasting checklist

The book says a dish is checked twice, on two different questions. This is how that works on
a page. **The two lists must not blur into one** — a checker who answers both questions
answers neither properly.

## The head chef's list — was the process followed?

Mechanical where possible, so it is evidence rather than opinion. Run by the print manager
before the page reaches the taster. Any failure here is sent back **without troubling the
taster at all** — a page that ignored the template does not deserve a reader.

1. The title matches `CONTENTS.md` exactly, number and name.
2. The **What this chapter settles:** line is present, and is one sentence.
3. `## The rules, flat` is present, numbered, and imperative.
4. **Every flat rule traces to something explained in the body.** A flat rule with no
   explanation above it is a promise nobody argued for. This is the one that matters most.
5. Body under 700 words, excluding the flat rules.
6. Zero words that only mean something to a programmer, in the body.
7. Nothing contradicts `CONTENTS.md`, `KNOWN-HOLES.md`, or any chapter already written.
8. It covers everything the running order gives this chapter, and nothing given to another.

## The taster's list — is the quality right?

Judgement, not measurement. Run only on pages that passed the head chef.

1. **Would someone who has never written a line of code understand this on one read?** Not
   "could they work it out". One read.
2. **Does it sound like chapters 0 and 1?** Those two are the voice standard: short
   declarative sentences, a statement for a heading, the reason attached to the claim in the
   same breath.
3. **Are the analogies only the agreed ones** — the chain, the restaurant, its kitchen, a
   dish, the chefs, the taster, the appliance, the recipe and the shopping list, the
   telephone game, a building with doors? An invented analogy is a fault however good it is.
4. **Is anything asserted that nobody established?** Invented mechanism, invented commands,
   invented history. Confident and unfounded is worse than absent.
5. **Does it earn its length?** Padding is a fault of its own. So is a chapter that hits the
   word limit by leaving out something it promised.

## The verdict

One of two words, and a reason either way.

- **SERVE** — it goes in the book.
- **SEND BACK** — with the specific fault named, and the sentence or section it is in. "It
  could be better" is not a verdict; it is a shrug, and it fails the taster, not the page.

Both verdicts are logged. So is the shadow taster's, which decides nothing and is recorded
beside it.

## The rule that outranks all of the above

**The taster never wrote the page it judges.** Not in this session, not in another. If the
only available taster wrote it, the page is not tasted today — and the kitchen does not serve
a dish nobody checked.
