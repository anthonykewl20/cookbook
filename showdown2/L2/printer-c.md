# ?. Serving on a busy night

**What this chapter settles:** how to keep serving through an ordinary rush without hiding a delayed check, and how to return to normal afterwards.

## A busy night belongs in the manual

Every kitchen has nights when orders arrive faster than usual. **A busy night is normal service, not an emergency.** It matters because those nights are often the nights that matter most to the business.

A manual that works only while the kitchen is quiet does not work. The rush must have a written way through it, so a cook does not have to invent one while customers are waiting.

## The pass can fill faster than it clears

Every finished dish reaches the pass for checking. Sometimes cooks finish dishes faster than the taster can judge them. The waiting line then grows even though everybody is doing the right job.

**A growing line is the signal to watch.** While the taster can keep up, every dish waits for its quality verdict before it leaves. Once the line grows past what the taster can keep up with, the busy-night rule may be used.

## A present taster can finish the checks later

**A crowded pass is not the same as having no checker.** The taster is still on duty and will give every required quality verdict. Only the time of that verdict changes. If no taster can make the check at all, the kitchen does not serve.

When the pass has grown beyond the taster's pace, do these things in order.

1. The head chef gives the dish its method verdict as usual.
2. The cook may send the dish to the customer and immediately add a factual note at the end of the operations log: this dish was served before tasting. That note records what happened. It does not approve the cook's own work.
3. At the end of service, the taster reads every such note, gives each dish its quality verdict, and adds that result at the end of the log.

The dish still receives two verdicts from two different heads. **The rush delays the taster's verdict; it does not remove it.** This keeps service moving without letting the cook become its own judge.

## The log keeps the rush in view

Every dish sent out before tasting is written into the operations log when it leaves. **Nothing served this way is allowed to disappear.** The record lets the taster find every delayed check at the end of service.

The original note is never changed. The later quality verdict is another note added at the end, because the log is the order in which things actually happened.

## Normal service returns with the quiet

The busy-night rule lasts only while the pass is growing beyond the taster's pace. **When the rush passes, every dish waits for tasting before it leaves again.** A temporary way through a crowded pass must not become the kitchen's ordinary habit.

## Three checks show what failed

If the kitchen has not caught up, check these things in order.

1. **A served dish has no note in the log.** The taster cannot find it for the delayed quality verdict.
2. **A delayed verdict is still missing at the end of service.** The taster has not finished catching up.
3. **Dishes still leave before tasting after the line has eased.** The kitchen has not returned to normal service.

## Never make the rush invisible

The tempting mistake is to serve first and promise to remember later. Do not. **An unrecorded dish cannot be found for its delayed verdict**, and the owner is left believing the ordinary checks happened when they did not.

Record the dish as it leaves. The rush changes when tasting happens. It never changes the truth of the log.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Treat a busy night as a normal condition the manual must survive.
2. While the taster can keep up, require its quality verdict before every dish leaves.
3. If the pass grows beyond the taster's pace, require the head chef's method verdict, serve the dish, and immediately add a factual served-before-tasting note at the end of the operations log.
4. Never treat the cook's factual note as a verdict on its own work.
5. At the end of service, require the taster to read every served-before-tasting note, give each delayed quality verdict, and add each result at the end of the log.
6. If no taster can make the required check at all, stop service.
7. When the rush passes, require the taster's quality verdict before every dish leaves again.
8. Never rewrite or edit the operations log; only add to its end.
