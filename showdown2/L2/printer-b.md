# 2. Serving on a busy night

**What this chapter settles:** how to keep the kitchen serving through an ordinary rush without hiding anything, and how to return to normal afterwards.

## A busy night is normal, not an emergency

Every restaurant has a rush. Orders arrive in a wave, dishes crowd the pass, and the taster cannot keep up. **A busy night is not a failure.** It is part of the job, and the manual must survive one.

Those nights are often the nights that matter most to the business. A manual that works only while the kitchen is quiet does not work. The rush must have a written way through it, so a cook does not have to invent one while customers are waiting.

## The pass gets crowded

Every finished dish reaches the pass for checking. The head chef gives the method verdict. The taster gives the quality verdict. When many dishes finish at once, **the taster becomes the pace that holds up the kitchen.** The queue grows even though everyone is doing the right job.

A growing queue is the signal to watch. While the taster can keep up, every dish waits for both verdicts. Once the line grows past what the taster can clear, the busy-night rule may be used.

## Keeping service moving

**A crowded pass is not the same as having no checker.** The taster is still on duty and will give every required quality verdict. Only the time of that verdict changes. No taster at all means no service, and that is a different problem.

When the pass has grown beyond the taster's pace, do these things in order.

1. The head chef gives the dish its method verdict as usual.
2. The cook sends the dish to the customer. The cook adds a factual note at the end of the operations log: this dish was served before tasting.
3. At the end of service, the taster reads the log, finds every dish served before tasting, and gives each one its delayed quality verdict. The taster adds each result at the end of the log.

The dish still receives two verdicts from two different heads. **The rush delays the taster's verdict; it does not remove it.** This keeps service moving without letting the cook become its own judge.

## Recording it

**Nothing served this way disappears.** Every dish sent out before tasting is written into the operations log when it leaves. The note records what happened: the dish left the kitchen.

The original note is never changed. The later quality verdict is another note added at the end. The log is the order in which things actually happened, and it is only ever added to.

## Returning to normal

The busy-night rule lasts only while the pass is growing beyond the taster's pace. **When the rush passes, every dish waits for tasting before it leaves again.**

A temporary way through a crowded pass must not become the kitchen's everyday habit. The ordinary rule — every dish checked before it leaves — returns as soon as the taster can keep up.

## What goes wrong

Check these three things, in this order.

1. **A served dish has no note in the log.** The taster cannot find it for the delayed quality verdict.
2. **A delayed verdict is still missing at the end of service.** The taster has not finished catching up.
3. **Dishes still leave before tasting after the rush has passed.** The kitchen has not returned to normal service.

## The one thing never to do

Do not turn a record that a dish was served into the taster's verdict. **What happened and whether it was right are different things.** The log records the first. The taster gives the second. Treating the note as an approval is tempting on a busy night, because it saves time. It also hides the very gap this chapter exists to keep visible.
