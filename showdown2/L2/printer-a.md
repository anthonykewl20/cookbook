# 2. Serving on a busy night

**What this chapter settles:** a busy night must stay visible and orderly, even when the pass is fuller than the taster can clear at once.

## A busy night is part of the job

Every restaurant has a rush. **A crowded pass is normal service, not an emergency.** The manual must work on the nights when the business most needs it, not only when the kitchen is quiet.

Do not pretend that a queue at the pass means the kitchen has failed. Dishes can arrive for the taster faster than they can be judged. The queue then grows. Seeing that truth clearly is better than hiding it, because you can only make a sound decision about a visible queue.

## The pass can become the slowest part of service

The taster has one job: to say whether the quality is right. That work takes time. When many dishes reach the pass together, **the taster can become the pace that holds up the kitchen.**

The intended busy-night practice is clear about what it tries to protect. Once the queue is beyond what the taster can keep up with, a cook would send a dish to the customer and record it as served. At the end of service, the taster would read the log and catch up on those dishes. This keeps service moving when the customers are waiting.

Every such departure would have to be recorded when it happens. The log is the diary of service. It says that the dish left the kitchen. It must not be changed later to make the rush look neater.

When the rush passes, the kitchen would return to its ordinary rhythm: each dish would be checked before it left. The late catch-up is for the rush only. It is not a new everyday way to run the pass.

## An unapproved shortcut breaks the published rules

**Sending a dish before the taster judges it conflicts with two rules already bound into this book.** Every dish is said to need the taster's verdict, and no required check means no service. A busy night is not named as an exception to either one.

Writing the shortcut as though it were already allowed would hide that conflict. Recording a dish as served does not make the taster's verdict appear. A factual note may say what happened. It cannot turn an unchecked dish into a checked one.

This matters most in a rush. The pressure to keep customers waiting less is real, which is why the shortcut is tempting. It is also the moment when a vague exception spreads fastest from one cook to the next.

## The one thing never to do is turn a record into a verdict

Do not call a dish approved because it was sent out and written into the log. That is tempting because the entry proves it left the kitchen. **What happened and whether it was right are different things.** The first belongs in the log. The second belongs to the taster.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Treat a busy night as normal service.
2. Append every event to the log in the order it happened.
3. Record any dish sent out during a rush as served when it leaves the kitchen.
4. Return to checking every dish before it leaves when the rush passes.
5. Never treat a record that a dish was served as the taster's verdict.
6. Do not authorise serving before the taster's verdict until the conflict below is resolved.

## Unresolved

The requested busy-night practice permits a dish to leave before the taster catches up. The published rules say that every dish needs the taster's verdict and that no required check means no service. The owner must decide whether a busy night is an exception, and state exactly what becomes of the required taster verdict before this practice can be made part of the manual.
