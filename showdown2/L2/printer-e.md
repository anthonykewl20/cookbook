# {{N}}. Serving on a busy night

**What this chapter settles:** how the kitchen keeps serving through the busiest night without dropping the rule that every dish is checked.

## A busy night is normal

Every kitchen has nights when the orders pile up, and the manual has to be able to live through one. **A rush is not a fire to put out.** It is a normal working night, and the rules are written to keep working when it comes. A book that only held together on quiet nights would be useless on the one night the business depends on.

## The pass fills up

The pass is the checking counter, where every dish is checked before it leaves. On a busy night, dishes arrive there faster than they can be checked, and a queue builds. **When the queue outruns the taster, the waiting itself slows the whole service**. The dishes are ready. The customers are waiting. The counter is full.

## Service keeps moving

When the queue at the pass outruns the taster, the cook may take each affected dish straight out to the customer and record it as served. The dish goes out first; the taster's check comes after.

This is permitted, not a break in the rules. **The check is moved to the end of service, not removed.** The taster still checks every one of those dishes — afterwards, by reading the log at the end of service. The kitchen keeps moving on the nights that matter most, because the dish no longer sits at the pass waiting for a check that can come later.

Nothing here lets the cook judge its own dish. **The cook records one fact only: that the dish was served** — not that it was good. And every dish sent this way is added at the end of the log — existing entries are never rewritten or edited, so nothing served is hidden.

## Checking returns to normal

A rush passes. When it does, **checking returns to normal — every dish is checked at the pass before it leaves again**, the way it is on any other night. When the queue is gone, the kitchen no longer uses the busy-night permission.

## The busy night runs in this order

1. The queue at the pass outruns the taster.
2. The cook sends each affected dish straight to the customer and records it as served.
3. When the rush passes, normal before-leaving checks resume.
4. At the end of service, the taster catches up by reading the log.

## Three checks find what went wrong

1. **A dish sent straight out is missing from the log.** During catch-up the taster has no entry to read for that dish.
2. **The taster has not caught up from the log at the end of service.** The whole permission rests on the check being moved, not dropped; a catch-up that never happens is a check that never happens.
3. **The rush has passed but direct service continues.** The permission is for the queue that outruns the taster. Once that queue is gone, sending dishes straight out is no longer the busy-night rule — it is just skipping the check.

## The one thing never to do

There is one trap on a busy night, and it is tempting.

Do not send a dish straight out without writing it into the log. The temptation is real: you are already skipping the wait at the pass, so skipping the writing feels like the same saving. It is not. The wait at the pass is caught up later; **a dish left unlogged leaves the taster with no entry to read during catch-up**.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Treat a busy night as a normal night, not an emergency.
2. When the queue outruns the taster, allow the cook to send an affected dish straight to the customer.
3. Add each such dish to the end of the log as served.
4. Keep the cook's entry factual; never treat it as a verdict.
5. At the end of service, have the taster catch up by reading the log.
6. When the rush passes, resume checking every dish before it leaves.
7. Never send a dish directly without logging it.
