# 2. Serving on a busy night

**What this chapter settles:** how to keep the kitchen moving when dishes reach the pass faster than the taster can check them, record every early dish, and return to checking when the rush passes.

## A busy night belongs in the manual

Every kitchen has busy nights. They are not emergencies. They are normal conditions that test whether the manual works when work arrives faster than usual.

**The rush must change the order of checking, not hide what happened.** The kitchen keeps moving. The record stays honest. The taster catches up when plates stop arriving.

## The pass is where the wait becomes visible

The pass is the checking counter. Finished dishes wait there before leaving. The taster checks each one while the queue is manageable.

On a busy night, dishes can arrive faster than the taster can check them. The queue grows. That is the sign to use the busy-night rule.

The taster must still be there. This is not a missing checker. **The dish still receives two verdicts from different heads:** the head chef says whether the recipe was followed, and the taster says whether the quality is right. Only timing changes.

## The cook may send a waiting dish out

When the queue has grown beyond what the taster can keep up with, the cook may send the waiting dish straight to the customer. Record it as served at once.

This keeps service moving on the nights that matter most to the business. It does not let the cook approve the cook's own dish. It gives the taster a clear list to catch up on.

## Every early dish enters the log

A dish sent out before the taster's check is not an exception to recording. **It is an event that must be written down because the log records what actually happened.**

Write every such dish into the operations log as served through the busy-night rule. Add the entry in the order the event happened. Do not rewrite it later to make the check appear earlier. The log is a diary. Only add to its end.

At the end of service, the taster reads the log and catches up on every dish that went out early. The delayed check is still made. A full pass is not an absent taster.

## The rush has a clear sequence

1. Keep the ordinary check in place while the taster can keep up.
2. When the pass grows beyond the taster's reach, send the waiting dish to the customer.
3. Record that dish as served in the operations log immediately.
4. At the end of service, have the taster read the log and catch up on every dish sent out this way.
5. When the rush passes, return to checking every dish before it leaves the kitchen.

## The record fails before the kitchen does

Check these failures first.

1. **A dish left without a log entry.** The taster cannot catch up on an event that was hidden.
2. **The log was tidied after the rush.** Rewriting an entry changes the order of what happened. It makes the record less trustworthy when you need it.
3. **The busy-night rule stayed on after the queue cleared.** A temporary way to keep service moving becomes a habit, and dishes leave unchecked even though the taster can keep up.
4. **A missing taster was treated as a full pass.** A taster who is catching up later is present. A required checker who is unavailable is different; no checker, no service.

## The one thing never to do

Never send a dish through the busy-night rule without recording it immediately. The rush makes writing feel like a delay, but an unwritten dish disappears from the taster's evening and the chain's account of what happened.

---

## The rules, flat

1. Treat a busy night as a normal condition the manual must handle.
2. Keep checking every dish at the pass while the taster can keep up.
3. When the pass outgrows the taster's ability to keep up, send the waiting dish straight to the customer.
4. Record every dish sent this way as served in the operations log immediately.
5. Add log entries at the end, in the order events happen. Never rewrite or edit the log.
6. At the end of service, have the taster read the log and check every dish sent out early.
7. When the rush passes, return to checking every dish before it leaves the kitchen.
