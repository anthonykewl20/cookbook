# 1. The interview

**What this chapter settles:** that before any work begins, you and the appliance agree on the words the chain will use — and that those words stay fixed once set.

## Words come first

Before a dish is ordered or a chef is hired, you and the appliance must mean the same thing by the same word. **Nothing can be agreed, checked, or handed over until that holds.** Every step after this checks “is this the dish?” against a word. If that word means two things to two people, the check passes and the dish is still wrong.

That is why this first part of the book is about language and nothing else. No staff are hired in it. No work is done in it. It is one conversation, and the conversation is about words.

## It is an interview, not a form

The appliance does not slide you a form full of blanks that someone else chose. **It sits with you and asks**, and the two of you find the words together.

You know what this place is for and how you picture it. The appliance knows what every later chapter will need each word to carry. Neither of you has the answer alone — the interview is where the two meet.

A form would press you into a stranger’s picture. An interview lets you bring your own.

## This book’s words are an example, not the law

This book says *restaurant*, *kitchen*, *dish*, *chef*, *taster*. **Those are one owner’s way of seeing**, and that owner happens to think in pictures.

Another owner may think in workshops, or studios, or plain blunt words with no picture behind them. All of them work, because the book does not need a picture — it needs one steady word for each thing that must be named. Publishing one owner’s pictures as law would impose them on every reader, and a picture that does not fit your place is worse than none.

Take what this book means by each word. Bring your own word for it, if you have one.

## Six things must be named

Six things must have an agreed word, or the rest of this book cannot be followed.

1. **Who owns the place** — the one the whole chain answers to.
2. **Who runs it day to day** — the one in charge of the kitchen each shift.
3. **Who does the work** — the chefs.
4. **Who checks that work** — the taster.
5. **The machine they all work on** — the appliance.
6. **One unit of work** — one dish.

Keep the book's example words, or bring your own. Everything else can wait; you do not need a word for everything before you begin.

## The words live in your kitchen, not in this book

The words you settle on are written down **in that kitchen, locally** — never back into this book. **The book ships blank on this and stays blank**, because the right words for your place are yours, and writing them into a shared book would make them look like everyone’s.

Your kitchen keeps its own short list of its own words. The book keeps only the rule that such a list must exist.

## Never bend a settled word

There is one thing to get wrong here, and it is quiet.

Once a word is agreed, it stops being negotiable. **Nobody coins a second word for the same thing, and nobody stretches an agreed word to cover something more convenient.** Both are tempting because both cost nothing in the moment — and both are how a shared language stops being shared without anyone noticing. A second word gives one thing two names. A stretched word lets one side win an argument by quietly widening what the word covers. The cure is not watching harder. The cure is that the words simply do not move.

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Agree the words before any work begins. Do not hire staff or do work in this first part.
2. Settle the words in an interview with the appliance. Do not fill in a form of blanks someone else chose.
3. Treat this book’s words — restaurant, kitchen, dish, chef, taster — as one owner’s example, not the law. Bring your own where you have them.
4. Name these six before following the book: who owns the place, who runs it day to day, who does the work, who checks that work, the machine they all work on, and one unit of work. Let everything else wait.
5. Write the agreed words down locally, in that kitchen. Never write them back into this book. Keep the book blank on them.
6. Fix each word once it is agreed. Do not coin a second word for the same thing, and do not stretch an agreed word into a more convenient meaning.
