# 1. The interview

**What this chapter settles:** the words your kitchen will use, where they belong, and how their meanings stay steady.

## Words come before work

Before anything can be agreed or checked or handed over, both sides must give each word the same meaning. **Shared words make agreement possible.** If “finished” means cooked to one side and ready to serve to the other, agreement sounds complete. If “checker” means the maker, the check is gone.

Part One does no work. It gives work names that survive a change of hands. Staff are strangers. Words must be constant.

## The appliance interviews you

The appliance sits with you, the owner, and asks questions. You answer and clarify. **You arrive at the words together.** You are not filling in blanks somebody else chose.

This is an interview, not a form.

It asks six questions. This happens before anyone is hired or any dish is made. It gives later conversations common words.

## This book shows one owner’s picture

This book uses restaurant words:

- restaurant
- kitchen
- dish
- chef
- taster

**They are a worked example, not the law.** They show one visually minded owner’s picture.

You may think in workshops, studios, or plain blunt terms with no picture at all. Choose any words you and the appliance understand. The interview travels from owner to owner. Its answers do not. Publishing one owner’s pictures as law would impose them on readers.

## Six names let the rest begin

Not everything needs a name today. **Name only what the rest of the book cannot discuss without it.**

Agree a word for:

1. the owner;
2. the person who runs the place day to day;
3. the people who do the work;
4. the person who checks the work;
5. the appliance, the machine they work on;
6. one unit of work.

These six are enough. Everything else waits.

## The conversation ends in a local list

Perform four moves in order.

1. Let the appliance ask what each of the six things is called in your kitchen.
2. Agree with it on one word for each.
3. Write the agreed words down locally, in that kitchen.
4. Treat that list as fixed language for later conversations.

The list belongs in that kitchen. **Never write your kitchen’s words back into this book.** It ships blank on them and stays blank because its examples must not become another owner’s law. Do not rely on memory. A word in one head changes when that person leaves.

## An agreed word does not wander

Agreement is not just choosing a label. It is keeping the label and its meaning. **Do not invent a competing word for the same thing later.** That makes two languages. Do not stretch an agreed word into a convenient meaning. That keeps the old label while removing the old promise.

The first handover that uses a word differently has already broken the shared language.

## What goes wrong

Check these failures in this order.

1. **A familiar word hides two meanings.** Define it for this kitchen, because familiarity is not agreement.
2. One of the six names is missing. Return to it, because later talk will have no shared word for that thing.
3. The book’s example is treated as an answer. Choose locally, because the book’s pictures belong to one owner.
4. A second word or stretched meaning appears. Keep the agreed word and meaning, because competing language is drift.

## Never borrow the picture as a verdict

Ready-made words feel faster. **Do not accept this book’s picture as law.** Use its words if they fit. Change them if they do not. The point is agreement with the appliance, not obedience to this owner’s picture.

## The rules, flat

1. Begin with words before hiring anyone or doing any work.
2. Let the appliance ask the owner questions.
3. Arrive at each word together with the appliance.
4. Treat restaurant, kitchen, dish, chef, and taster as one owner’s worked example, not universal law.
5. Agree words for the owner, the day-to-day runner, the doers, the checker, the appliance, and one unit of work.
6. Write the agreed words down locally in that kitchen.
7. Leave this book blank about local words.
8. Keep one agreed word for each thing.
9. Keep each agreed word’s meaning fixed.
