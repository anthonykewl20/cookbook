# 1. The interview

**What this chapter settles:** how to agree on words before any work begins, so that every stranger who walks in already knows what everything is called.

## Words come first

Someone says "the dish" and means one plate. Someone else hears it and thinks it means the whole order. Neither knows they disagree until the wrong thing arrives.

**Nothing can be agreed, checked, or handed over until both sides mean the same thing by the same word.** This is not carefulness. It is the difference between a chain where every kitchen plates the same dish and one where every kitchen invents its own.

Words come first because every rule after this chapter — who tastes a dish, who writes the recipe, who is allowed to change anything — will be written in these words. If the words are loose, the rules are loose. A rule that is loose is already broken.

## The interview is a conversation, not a form

When you open a new kitchen, the appliance does not hand you a blank to fill in. **It asks you questions, and the two of you arrive at the words together.**

The questions are open. Who owns this place? Who runs it day to day? What do we call one unit of work? The appliance asks because it needs a shared language before it can enforce a shared process, and strangers cannot share a language they have not spoken aloud. You are not ticking boxes. You are not choosing from a list. You are naming your own kitchen, in your own words, and the appliance is listening.

## One owner's words are not every owner's words

This book says *restaurant*, *kitchen*, *dish*, *chef*, *taster*. Those are one owner's way of seeing the work, and that owner thinks in pictures. Another owner might call them *workshop*, *studio*, *piece*, *maker*, *reviewer*. A third might keep it blunt: *project*, *workspace*, *task*, *worker*, *checker*.

**Any of them works. None of them is the law.** Publishing one owner's pictures as law would impose them on every reader, and the book's job is to give you the process, not the poetry. The words in this book are a worked example. You read them to understand the shape of the thing. Then you replace them with your own.

## Six things need a name now

Some words can wait. **Six cannot, or the rest of the book cannot be followed.** The appliance will ask for each one. It tells you what the thing does, and you tell it the word.

1. **Who owns the chain.** The person the book is written to.
2. **Who runs a kitchen day to day.** The person who decides which dishes get cooked and when to release them.
3. **Who does the work.** The cooks — the ones who take a recipe and deliver a finished dish.
4. **Who checks the dish before it leaves.** The taster. Nobody serves a dish they tasted themselves.
5. **The machine everyone works on.** The appliance — where the recipes are kept and the work is done.
6. **One unit of work.** The dish. What a cook picks up and delivers complete.

Everything else — sections of the kitchen, kinds of recipes, who is allowed to hire — can wait. Those are named in the chapter where they are first needed. Naming everything on the first day is painting every door in a building you have not yet walked through.

## The words live in the kitchen, not in this book

**The book ships blank on this and stays blank.** The words you and the appliance agree on are written down in your own kitchen, in a single place, and they never come back into these pages.

There is a reason. The moment your words appear in two places, one copy begins to drift. Somebody updates the local copy and forgets the book. Somebody reads the book and never checks the local copy. This is not carelessness — it is what always happens when one truth has two homes. The only defence is to give it one.

## The words are fixed once agreed

Two things destroy a shared language, and neither looks like destruction at the time.

The first is inventing a competing word. Somebody joins the kitchen, prefers their own term, and uses it in conversation. Nobody corrects them. Months later, two words exist for the same thing, and new staff hear one or the other at random.

The second is stretching an agreed word. Somebody decides that "dish" now also means a collection of dishes, because it is convenient. The word grows wider, its meaning gets thinner, and one day the appliance uses the word as it was agreed and the humans use it as it has stretched and neither side notices.

**Once the words are agreed, they are fixed.** Nobody invents a synonym. Nobody borrows a word for a wider job. The door for changing them is described in Part Three, and it is one door — not six, not a back way, not "just this once." A chain has exactly as many standards as it has doors for changing them, and one door is the right number.

8. The single door for changing agreed words is described in Part Three. There is no other door.
