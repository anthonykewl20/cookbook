# 1. The interview

**What this chapter settles:** how you and the appliance choose the few names that let you
speak about your chain without guessing.

## Names are the first agreement

Before anything can be agreed, checked, or handed over, you and the appliance must mean the
same thing by the same word. **A promise cannot be kept if its important words mean different
things to each side.**

That is how the telephone game starts. One cook hears one meaning. The next cook hears another.
Both may be trying their best, yet neither can tell that the other has changed the promise.
Words come first because they give every later promise something firm to stand on.

## The appliance asks and you decide together

**This is an interview, not a form.** The appliance sits with you and asks questions. You
answer in the words that fit your chain. Together, you arrive at the names.

You are not filling blank spaces chosen by somebody who has never seen your kitchen. The
questions make sure the necessary things are not missed. The answers remain yours.

## The book's names are a picture, not the law

This book says restaurant, kitchen, dish, chef, and taster. **They are a worked example, not a
set of names you must inherit.** They suit one owner who thinks in pictures.

You may think in workshops. You may think in studios. You may prefer plain, blunt names with
no picture in them. That is all right. A name is useful when you and the appliance share it,
not when it sounds like this book.

## Six things are enough to name today

Do not try to name every room in the building before you enter it. Name only what the rest of
the book needs.

1. Name the person who owns the place.
2. Name the person who runs it from day to day.
3. Name the people who do the work.
4. Name the person who checks the work.
5. Name the machine they work on.
6. Name one piece of work.

Once each of these has an agreed name, you have enough to continue. Everything else can wait
until it becomes useful.

## Put your names in your own kitchen

Write the agreed names down locally, with this kitchen. **They belong to the chain that chose
them.** The book ships blank on this and stays blank, because it cannot choose your language
without choosing it for you.

Do not carry your names back into the book. Another owner needs the same freedom to arrive at
names that fit their own place.

## A shared word must keep its shape

Once a word is agreed, keep it fixed. Do not invent a second word for the same thing later.
Do not stretch the agreed word to cover a more convenient thing either.

Both mistakes feel small when they happen. One sounds like a helpful new label. The other
sounds like saving time. **Both make the shared language quietly stop being shared.** The next
stranger then has to guess which word, or which meaning, the kitchen meant.

## Most trouble begins with a familiar word

Check these in this order.

1. **You treated the book's picture as a law.** Return to the interview and choose the names
   that fit your chain.
2. **One of the six things has no agreed name.** Settle that name before continuing, because
   the next conversation will need it.
3. **Two words now point at one thing, or one word points at two things.** Restore the original
   agreement before the telephone game carries the change further.

## Never let the book choose for you

It is tempting to borrow restaurant, kitchen, dish, chef, and taster because they are already
on the page. Do not let that save you the interview. **A borrowed picture can hide a different
meaning**, and a hidden difference is exactly what the interview is there to find.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance
obeys.*

1. Hold an interview with the appliance before agreeing, checking, or handing over work.
2. Agree one word for the owner, the day-to-day runner, the people who do the work, the
   checker, the appliance, and one piece of work.
3. Treat this book's restaurant words as an example, never as required names.
4. Write the agreed words locally in your kitchen. Keep them out of this book.
5. Use one agreed word for one thing. Do not add competing words or stretch a word into a new
   meaning.
