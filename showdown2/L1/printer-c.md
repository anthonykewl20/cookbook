# 1. The interview

**What this chapter settles:** the few shared words you and the appliance must agree before anything else can begin.

## Shared words come before work

Before anyone can agree, check or hand over a dish, **you and the appliance must mean the same thing by the words you use.**

If a dish means one request to you and a whole run of work to somebody else, agreement is only apparent. A check can pass the wrong thing. A handover can carry a different promise from the one you made. The people will change, so a shared language cannot live in their memories. It must be settled before any work begins.

## The appliance interviews you

This first conversation is an interview, not a form. **The appliance asks questions, and the two of you arrive at the words together.** You are not filling in blanks somebody else chose.

The appliance brings the need for clear names. You bring the meaning those names must carry. Neither side quietly decides for the other. No one is hired and no dish is made during this conversation. It settles language only.

## This book's pictures are examples, not law

This book speaks of a restaurant, kitchen, dish, chef and taster. **Those words are one owner's worked example.** That owner happens to think in pictures.

You may see a workshop or a studio instead. You may prefer blunt words with no picture in them at all. Either is sound when each word has one agreed meaning. Publishing this owner's pictures as law would impose one way of seeing on every owner, even where it does not fit.

## Only six things need names now

Do not name the whole world. **Agree on a word for each thing the rest of this book cannot do without:**

1. the person who owns the place;
2. the person who runs it day to day;
3. the person who does the work;
4. the person who checks the work;
5. the machine they will all work on; and
6. one unit of work.

This is a list of meanings, not a list of required answers. Your words may use this book's pictures, another set of pictures or none at all. Everything beyond these six can wait. Naming more now would settle distinctions you have not yet needed.

## The agreement belongs in your kitchen

**Write the agreed words down in the kitchen where they will be used.** That gives every future person the same place to learn what you mean.

Never write your agreement back into this book. The book ships blank on these choices because each owner must make them. It stays blank so one owner's language never becomes everybody else's law.

## Never let the words drift

Once the words are agreed, **keep both each word and its meaning fixed.** Do not invent a second word for the same thing. A future person may reasonably think two words name two different things.

Do not stretch an agreed word to cover something more convenient. Old promises and new ones would then use the same word for different things. Both changes are tempting because they can make one conversation easier. Both quietly end the shared language that every later conversation depends on.

---

## The rules, flat

*The same rules as above, with the explanation removed. This is the part the appliance obeys.*

1. Interview the owner and agree the shared words before any hiring or work begins.
2. Ask questions and arrive at the words with the owner. Never make the owner fill in language chosen in advance.
3. Treat restaurant, kitchen, dish, chef and taster as one owner's worked example, never as required words.
4. Agree words for the owner, the day-to-day runner, the worker, the checker, the machine and one unit of work. Leave every other name until it is needed.
5. Write the agreement in the owner's kitchen. Never write it back into this book; keep the book blank on every owner's choices.
6. Use one fixed word with one fixed meaning for each agreed thing. Never add a competing word or stretch an agreed meaning.
