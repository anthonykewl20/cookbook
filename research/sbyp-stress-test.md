# Stress-testing "Settle by proof, not by who's speaking"

An adversarial pass. The rule under test (the chapter's working claim):

> Authority may PROPOSE a claim but not CLOSE a proof-gated one. Proof-gated claims
> (contested / high-stakes / hard-to-reverse) close only with (1) an acceptance test, sanity-checked
> by someone other than its author, named BEFORE the answer is produced, that the answer is run
> against and PASSES, and (2) confirmation by someone independent of BOTH the author and the owner.
> A routine, cheap-to-reverse claim closes on a CITED source and reopens as proof-gated the moment
> it is contested. Expertise is legitimate evidence, not proof.

The job here is to find where it breaks, not to defend it. Each objection is stated fairly first,
then ruled: **answerable** (the rule, or the chapter, can close it) or **a genuine limit to NAME**
(the chapter must put it on the page rather than hide it). Evidence is drawn from the book's own
measured record where the book has one — that is stronger than any external analogy.

The evidence base for the rule itself lives in `research/proof-over-authority.md`. The two findings
that matter most for this pass: pre-committing the test removes ~86% of flattering-but-false wins
(57%→8%, Kaplan & Irvin — MEASURED), and a single independent dissenter collapses conformity error
(Asch 37%→5% — MEASURED). The rule is not aspirational; its clauses map onto measured mechanisms.
That makes the objections below sharper, not weaker — a rule with evidence behind it has further to
fall when the evidence also exposes its edge.

---

## The sharpest objection, first

**(a) The independence bottleneck is structural at the book's own floor — and the rule, as written,
demands a confirmer the smallest kitchen may not contain.** This is the one that most threatens the
rule's fitness for the only audience the book has: the smallest place that still works.

### Stated fairly

The book's floor is three pairs of hands inside the kitchen — maker, checker, watcher — plus the
owner standing outside (`CONTENTS.md`, Part Three). The separations are load-bearing: the maker
never approves, the taster never cooked it, the doctor never treats, and the owner is explicitly
*outside* the building. `FOR-THE-OWNER.md` decision 3 confirmed that the head chef and the taster
must be two heads — one head answering both verdicts "would collapse chapter 19's four-outcome
table." So inside one kitchen we have roughly: a cook, a head chef, a taster, a doctor. The owner is
outside and, per this rule, *barred* from being the confirmer ("independent of BOTH the author and
the owner").

Now the rule demands, for every proof-gated claim, a confirmer independent of the author **and** the
owner. In a kitchen of four bodies, who is that? The head chef is often the author of the contested
claim (the head chef runs the place and raises the most faults, per ch. 25). If the head chef
authored it, the confirmer must be neither head chef nor owner — leaving the taster, the doctor, or
the cook. Often workable. But the rule needs this *for every proof-gated claim*, and a claim about
the taster's own checklist, or the doctor's own diagnosis, leaves no unconflicted body in the
building. The book's own answer to "who checks the doctor?" is "not a fourth role, or you never stop
hiring — the chain ends at the owner." But the owner is precisely the one this rule forbids. So at
the smallest scale, the rule can ask for a confirmer the kitchen structurally cannot produce.

This is not a hypothetical slipped in from outside. The book has already hit this wall once: the
owner's own proposal (give the method verdict to the taster, because its independence is *structural
not situational*) was put to a three-seat panel and rejected 3–0 (`FOR-THE-OWNER.md` #3). The
rejection was correct, but note what it cost: settling one contested claim consumed a *three-seat
panel including a seat briefed to argue for it*. The smallest kitchen does not run a three-seat panel
per claim. The wall is real and the book has the receipt for hitting it.

### Ruling: **genuine limit to NAME — but the rule is not a luxury, because its weak form always holds.**

Three things are true at once, and the chapter must say all three rather than pick the flattering
ones.

1. **The rule's weak form is free and always applies.** "Authority may propose but not close" costs
   nothing and needs no third person. A kitchen that cannot field a confirmer still gets the full
   benefit of the anti-authority clause: the head chef's say-so no longer settles the question. That
   alone is most of the win — the measured Milgram datum is that ~65% defer to authority against
   their own judgment, and the clause kills that without hiring anyone.

2. **The confirmer is usually an existing role, not a new body.** The rule says "independent of the
   author and the owner," not "a fifth role." In the common case — the cook authored, the head chef
   authored, even the doctor authored — one of the other separations already provides the confirmer
   (the taster, the doctor, the other checker). The rule slots into separations the kitchen already
   mandates. It only fails when *every* in-kitchen checker is conflicted on the specific claim.

3. **When no confirmer exists, the claim stays OPEN — it does not get closed, and that is the
   fallback.** This is the limit, and it is the honest one. The book already has the exact pattern:
   "no checker, no service" (ch. 9, ch. 19) closes the kitchen rather than serve an unchecked dish.
   The claim-scale version writes itself: **a proof-gated claim with no available confirmer is logged
   and stays open; it is not closed by authority, and the question slows or stops on that one
   claim.** The chain scale then dissolves the limit — ch. 40 makes two restaurants "a measuring
   instrument for the manual," and the area manager (ch. 38) is the confirmer who stands above any
   one kitchen. The rule's strong form is a *chain-scale* promise; at single-restaurant scale it is
   the weak form plus an honest "this one waits."

What the chapter must not do: ship the strong form ("a confirmer independent of author and owner")
as if the smallest kitchen can always field one. It cannot. The honest page says the confirmer is
usually already in the kitchen, that when it is not the claim waits open, and that the chain is what
makes the strong form universally available. A rule the smallest kitchen "cannot fully meet yet" is
exactly how growth is described all through Part Four — and the book's own stance is that the taster
is "not a luxury bought later." The chapter has to reconcile those two sentences explicitly, or it
hands the reader a contradiction.

## (b) Who decides the stakes — isn't "is this proof-gated?" itself an authority call?

### Stated fairly

The gate is "contested / high-stakes / hard-to-reverse." Somebody has to judge whether a claim
qualifies. That judgment is itself unchecked: the head chef who declares "this is routine, it just
needs a cited source" is exercising exactly the authority the rule is meant to displace. And the
owner who declares "this is high-stakes, proof-gate it" can weaponize the gate to stall work they
dislike. The gate is supposed to protect against authority; judging the gate looks like authority
all the way down.

### Ruling: **mostly answerable — one half is structurally protected, the other half has objective anchors, and a small residual remains to name.**

The "contested" half is the easy one, and the rule is already biased in the safe direction. "It
reopens as proof-gated the moment it is contested" means a single voice triggers the gate — and ch.
25 already lets *any* employee raise a fault. So "contested" needs no authority to adjudge it: one
cook, one waiter, one dishwasher saying "I don't buy it" forces proof-gating. The Asch dissenter
result (37%→5%) is precisely the measured case that the dissenter needs no authority, only
existence. The only way to game "contested" is to flood it — to contest everything and drain
attention (this returns in (e)) — which is a denial-of-service, not an evasion of proof. Evasion
runs the other way: declaring a contested claim "routine" does not survive the first dissent.

The "high-stakes / hard-to-reverse" half, when *nobody* has contested it, is the genuine judgment
call — and here the chapter can anchor to objective triggers the book already owns, rather than to
opinion. A claim is high-stakes, regardless of how it feels, if it touches any of:
- the **one door** — anything that amends a travelling instruction is high-stakes by definition
  (the whole chain inherits it);
- **money, credentials, the safe, the doorman** (ch. 8, ch. 13) — the book already treats these as
  protected;
- anything stamped into the **operations log** as a verdict (ch. 12), because the log is the
  restaurant's memory and a false entry there is hard-to-reverse in the exact sense ch. 12 forbids
  rewriting.

"Hard-to-reverse" has a mechanical test the chapter can state: *can it be undone in one step through
the one door, with nothing else depending on it?* If yes, routine. If no, proof-gated. That removes
most of the judgment.

The **residual to name**: a claim can be load-bearing and still pass all three objective tests — a
one-line recipe change that is cheap to revert but silently changes every dish. Blast radius is not
the same as reversibility (this returns in (c)). And someone still has to *apply* the tests, and
applying them is a judgment. The chapter's honest answer is that this residual judgment is small,
that the "reopens when contested" backstop catches mis-judgements in the safe direction, and that
the doctor (ch. 26) watches for the pattern of a kitchen that keeps mis-classifying. It is a limit;
it is a small one and it leans toward safety.

## (c) The routine-claim loophole — can a load-bearing claim be smuggled through as "routine"?

### Stated fairly

"Routine, cheap-to-reverse claim closes on a cited source" is a fast lane. A motivated author —
especially an authoritative one — can declare a load-bearing claim "cheap to reverse," close it on a
thin citation, and move on before anyone notices. The "reopens when contested" clause is the stated
fix. Is it enough, given that contesting takes effort and silence is the default?

### Ruling: **mostly answerable — with one specific gap the chapter must close by name.**

The fix is largely sufficient for the reason in (b): contesting is cheap and protected, and one
voice reopens the gate. The book's whole ch. 25 exists to make raising a fault expected rather than
exceptional, and "a fault seen but not recorded is itself a failure" — so the silent agreement the
loophole needs is the exact failure mode the book is built against. The clause also makes the
routine path *reversible by construction*: a routine close is never final, so the worst case is that
a bad claim lives for one shift and is reopened the moment someone reads it with fresh eyes.

The **gap to name**: the rule keys the fast lane to *cheap-to-reverse*, and a claim can be cheap to
reverse and still have a chain-wide blast radius. "Reversibility" measures how hard it is to undo;
"load-bearing" measures how much it touches when it lands. They are different axes. A one-line
change to a recipe every restaurant prints is cheap to revert in the file and expensive in the
consequence — and the manual-change case is exactly where the fast lane is most dangerous, because
the change inherits across the chain before anyone contests it. The chapter's fix writes itself from
the book's own spine: **anything that goes through the one door — anything that touches a travelling
instruction — is proof-gated on blast radius, not on reversibility, regardless of how small the edit
is.** `CONTENTS.md` already says the manual "changes when the owner changes it, and at no other time
and by no other hand"; settling what the manual *should* say is the highest-stakes claim there is.
Name the gap and the fast lane cannot carry a chain-affecting claim.

## (d) A test named in advance can still be rigged, trivial, or mis-specified — and the book has
measured its own guard failing.

### Stated fairly

The rule's first closure condition is an acceptance test, "sanity-checked by someone other than its
author, named BEFORE the answer." A test named up front defeats after-the-fact moving goalposts
(the Kaplan & Irvin result). But a test can be honest in timing and worthless in content: trivially
easy ("does it run?"), rigged to the author's preferred answer, or simply mis-specified so that the
wrong answer passes. The guard against this is "sanity-checked by someone other than its author."
Is that guard sufficient?

### Ruling: **genuine limit to NAME — the most evidenced one in this pass. The guard as stated is necessary and not sufficient, and the book has its own receipts proving it.**

This is where the book's own measured record is decisive, and it cuts against the rule as written.

- **`THE-CONTRADICTION-PASS.md`** proved that a second-person sanity check *certified a broken page
  5/5, twice* — and the second time the checker was told exactly what to look for, given the broken
  rule verbatim, and told the item mattered. The wording was never the fault; **the shape of the
  question was.** A check that returns one verdict for a whole page cannot find a fault that lives
  in one sentence.
- **Ticket T-06** (and `KNOWN-HOLES.md`) proved that a deleted load-bearing word — the word *"only"*
  — passed the taster (SERVE 5/5) and the rule-by-rule sweep, *both twice*. "A verdict-shaped check
  cannot see a missing word."

So "sanity-checked by someone other than its author" is empirically *insufficient* in this very
project. A second person, asked a holistic question, certifies a bad answer. The guard the rule
names is exactly the guard the book watched fail. Pre-committing the test (the rule's strongest
clause, and the best-measured mechanism) does not help here either: you can pre-commit a trivial
test ("does it compile?") on Tuesday and pass a broken answer on Wednesday, honestly and uselessly.

What the book measured to actually work — and what the chapter must therefore build into the rule's
first condition, not leave as an aside:

1. **Decompose the test until each sub-question has one place to hide.** The contradiction pass
   works rule-by-rule ("quote the sentence that makes THIS rule untrue, or say NONE"), not with one
   verdict for a whole claim. The rule's "an acceptance test" should read "an acceptance test
   decomposed to one verdict per load-bearing sub-claim." Pre-registration of a holistic test is the
   failure mode the book already caught.
2. **Run a seeded control.** The contradiction pass runs on a clean page *and* a deliberately broken
   one; `press/control-ch12-seeded.md` is now an entrance exam with a known answer. A test that has
   never caught a planted fault has not been shown to catch a real one. The chapter can require that
   a brand-new acceptance test be probed against at least one seeded wrong answer before it is
   trusted to pass a real one.
3. **A missing answer is a failure mode, not only a wrong one.** T-06 is the deletion case: the
   thing that passes every check is the *absence* of a load-bearing word. An acceptance test asks
   "is X present and correct?" and never "is anything that should be here *missing*?" The reader's
   chair finding (the pass-counter deletion, in `FOR-THE-OWNER.md` #1) is the same lesson: "only
   'what is missing?' finds a deletion."

The chapter does not need to abandon the clause; it needs to strengthen it with what the book
already learned the hard way. The honest page says: a test named in advance is necessary but not
sufficient; it must be decomposed, and a new one must be probed against a seeded fault; and the
hardest answer to catch is the one that is silently absent. If the chapter ships only "a test,
sanity-checked by someone other than its author," it ships the exact guard this book has twice
watched pass a broken page.

## (e) Does proof-gating cost more attention than the book can afford?

### Stated fairly

The book's scarcest resource is the owner's attention (`CONTENTS.md`, Part Four: "attention is the
only thing in a restaurant that cannot be bought in bulk"). Proof-gating has a fixed per-claim cost:
write the test, find the confirmer, run the answer, log it. Applied widely, it could paralyze the
exact routine work the kitchen depends on, and it concentrates cost on the smallest kitchen, which
has the least attention to spare. Where is the calibration that stops it grinding routine work to a
halt?

### Ruling: **answerable — the calibration exists, but it shares a residual with (a).**

The calibration is already in the rule, and the book supplies the rest.

- **The routine / proof-gated split IS the calibration.** Most claims are routine and close on a
  cited source — one lookup, no third person, no test. The full cost is borne only by the claim that
  *earns* it (contested, high-stakes, or hard-to-reverse), not spread over routine work. The cost is
  targeted, not blanket.
- **Attention-strain is itself a growth trigger.** Part Four's rule is that growth is triggered by
  "someone running out of attention, not work running out." If proof-gating (or anything else)
  saturates the head chef's attention, that is the measured signal to add a cook, then an area
  manager. The rule does not violate the attention principle; it *uses* the principle to know when
  the cost has grown too large for the current kitchen.
- **"No checker, no service" already accepts that some attention cost is non-negotiable.** The book
  closes the kitchen rather than serve an unchecked dish. The claim-scale version is milder: a
  proof-gated claim waits rather than closes on authority. That is slower, deliberately, and the
  alternative is the failure the book exists to prevent.

The **residual** is the same one as (a), and it is honest: at the smallest scale, a run of
proof-gated claims can saturate the kitchen's thin bench of confirmers, and the only answers are to
let some claims wait open (logged, not closed) or to slow the work. There is also a denial-of-service
shape — contest everything to drain attention — and the book's answer to that is ch. 26: the doctor
watches for *patterns*, and a person or a role that keeps raising frivolous contests is itself a
diagnosis the doctor makes. A frivolous contest is a fault like any other, and it goes in the log.

## Verdict — the defensible form after stress, and the limits the chapter must carry on the page

The rule survives. Its clauses map onto measured mechanisms (pre-commitment 57%→8%; one dissenter
37%→5%; independent inspection 57–82%), and the objections that land do not kill the rule — they
sharpen it and force three limits into the open. The book's own standard (`ch. 50`, `KNOWN-HOLES.md`,
`FOR-THE-OWNER.md`) is that a page names its limits rather than hiding them. A chapter titled "settle
by proof" that hid *these* edges would be committing the fault it exists to prevent.

**Three limits the chapter must NAME, not bury:**

1. **The confirmer is usually in the kitchen but not always — and when it is not, the claim waits
   open.** The strong form (a confirmer independent of author and owner) is fully available at chain
   scale (ch. 40's two-restaurant instrument, ch. 38's area manager). At single-restaurant scale it
   is the weak form ("authority may propose but not close") plus an honest "this one stays open and
   logged until an independent confirmer exists." The chapter reconciles this explicitly with the
   book's stance that the taster is "not a luxury bought later," or it hands the reader a
   contradiction.

2. **"An acceptance test, sanity-checked by a non-author" is necessary and not sufficient — this
   book has measured it failing.** The chapter strengthens the clause with what the book learned:
   decompose the test to one verdict per load-bearing sub-claim; probe a new test against a seeded
   wrong answer; and test for what is *missing*, not only for what is present. `THE-CONTRADICTION-
   PASS.md` and T-06 are the evidence. Shipping the clause as-is ships the guard this book twice
   watched pass a broken page.

3. **Blast radius, not reversibility, gates the fast lane for anything that touches a travelling
   instruction.** A cheap-to-revert edit that inherits across the chain is proof-gated on what it
   touches, not on how small the diff is. The one door is the brightest line the book has.

**Two residuals small enough to carry as a sentence each:**

- Judging "high-stakes" when nobody has contested it is a small, residual authority call; the
  objective triggers (the one door, money, credentials, the log) cover most of it, the "reopens when
  contested" backstop covers mis-judgement in the safe direction, and the doctor watches for the
  pattern of a kitchen that keeps getting it wrong.
- Proof-gating can be weaponized to drain attention (contest everything); the answer is that a
  frivolous contest is itself a fault, it goes in the log, and the doctor sees the pattern.

The rule's strongest clause — pre-commit the test — is also its best-measured one and the one no
objection here touches. If the chapter can carry the three limits honestly, "settle by proof" holds
up under the book's own scrutiny, which is the only scrutiny the book accepts.
