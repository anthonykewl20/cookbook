# Settle-by-proof — the PRIOR ART facet

Research leg for the chapter that carries the house rule: *"Settle contested claims by proof, not
by who's speaking. Authority may PROPOSE a claim but may not CLOSE a proof-gated one… Expertise is
legitimate evidence, not proof."*

The job of THIS file: show that "settle by proof, not authority" is **well-trodden convention**, not
a novelty the book is inventing — by naming the established institutions that already run on it, with
primary/authoritative citations. The chapter's claim is stronger if it can point to a 360-year-old
lineage than if it asserts the rule from nothing.

This file is in two batches. The **first batch** (the measured evidence base — pre-registration, the
designated dissenter, independent inspection, EBM) was cut short by an API rate limit after its early
findings; its substance was completed in two sibling files and is only *pointed to* here so this file
does not duplicate it. The **second batch** (below) is the four institutional/historical facets the
first batch never reached.

## First batch (already covered — do not re-derive here)

The measured-evidence leg lives in **`research/proof-over-authority.md`** (with the studies verified
against primary sources in **`research/sbyp-studies.md`**). What that batch established, in one line
each, so the second batch does not repeat it:

- **Pre-registration / registered reports** — committing the acceptance test before seeing the result
  made flattering-but-false positive findings fall from 57% to 8% (Kaplan & Irvin 2015, NHLBI trials).
  MEASURED. https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0132382
- **The designated dissenter** — one independent ally collapsed conformity error from ~37% to ~5%
  (Asch). MEASURED. Deference to authority is the Milgram datum (~65% full obedience).
- **Independent inspection** — a second, non-author reader finds a large fraction of defects the author
  missed (Fagan ~82%; broader syntheses ~57–60%, range 20–93%). MEASURED.
- **Evidence-based medicine** — proof improves outcomes, but the most-studied domain finds proof works
  best *with* expert judgment, not *instead of* it. This is the qualifier behind "expertise is evidence,
  not proof."
- **The appeal-to-authority fallacy** — the logic tradition's normative rule that a claim is not made
  true by who asserts it; legitimate expert consensus is *evidence*, the fallacy is treating it as *proof*.

## Second batch — four established institutions that already run on the rule

Each entry: the established practice, a primary/authoritative citation, and ONE line on what it adds
to the chapter's claim that "settle by proof, not authority" is convention, not invention.

### 2.1 The engineering codes' paramountcy clause — a profession installs ONE supreme rule

**The practice.** The mature engineering professions each codify a single rule that outranks every
other duty — including duties to client and employer — and they put it first, in identical language.
The **NSPE Code of Ethics for Engineers** opens its Fundamental Canons: *"Engineers, in the fulfillment
of their professional duties, shall: **1. Hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the
public.**"* The **IEEE Code of Ethics** opens its tenets the same way: *"**1. to hold paramount the
safety, health, and welfare of the public**…"* The same paramountcy clause appears across the US
engineering societies (ASCE, ASME, AIChE). The load-bearing word is **"paramount"**: NSPE's Board of
Ethical Review treats Canon I as the supreme rule that overrides Canon IV (act as a faithful agent /
client confidentiality) when the two conflict — the engineer's obligation is to the public, not to the
party paying them, and the code says so by *rank ordering* its rules, not by balancing them.

**Citations.**
- NSPE, *Code of Ethics for Engineers*, Fundamental Canons, Canon I ("Hold paramount the safety,
  health, and welfare of the public"). National Society of Professional Engineers.
  https://www.nspe.org/sites/default/files/resources/pdfs/Ethics/EthicsReferenceGuide.pdf
- IEEE, *IEEE Code of Ethics*, tenet 1 ("to hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the
  public"). Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers.
  https://www.ieee.org/about/corporate/governance/p7-8.html
- On Canon I overriding Canon IV (the faithful-agent duty) when they conflict: NSPE Board of Ethical
  Review case law, summarized in engineering-ethics treatments of the Canon I / Canon IV tension.

**What it adds to the chapter.** A profession's signature move is to install **one rule above all the
others and say so out loud** — exactly what the book does when it makes "the taster is never the cook"
invariant 5 and the two-verdicts rule invariant 11. The chapter's "settle by proof, not authority" is
the *claims*-domain analogue of engineering's paramountcy clause: a single supreme rule that outranks
rank itself. The book is not inventing the move of having a top rule; it is applying a move the
engineering profession settled on a century ago.

### 2.2 Mertonian scientific norms (CUDOS) — the institutional ethos of "settle on evidence, not status"

**The practice.** In 1942 the sociologist Robert K. Merton codified the institutional ethos of modern
science as four imperatives, later remembered by the acronym **CUDOS**:

- **Communalism** (Merton's word was "communism") — scientific findings are a common heritage; the
  producer's only "property" is recognition, not ownership. Secrecy is the antithesis.
- **Universalism** — truth-claims are to be "subjected to pre-established impersonal criteria
  regardless of the personal or social attributes of their protagonist." "Objectivity precludes
  particularism." A claim is judged on its evidence, not on the race, nationality, status, or
  reputation of who advanced it.
- **Disinterestedness** — the institution of science is organized so scientists act for the common
  enterprise, not for personal gain; Merton argued the "virtual absence of fraud" in science is not
  explained by individual virtue but by the institutional demand for *verifiability* and "rigorous
  policing" by peers.
- **Organized skepticism** — the "temporary suspension of judgement and detached scrutiny"; claims are
  subjected to shared, institutionalized critical scrutiny *before* being accepted.

**Which norm maps to "expertise is evidence, not proof"?** The phrase sits at the seam of **two**
norms. **Universalism** is the rule that a claim's acceptance turns on impersonal criteria, not on the
status of the speaker — that is the chapter's "not by who's speaking." **Organized skepticism** is the
institutional mechanism that says even an expert's claim must survive scrutiny before it *closes* —
that is the chapter's "not proof." So "expertise is evidence, not proof" = universalism (status does
not count as a closer) + organized skepticism (even expertise must be tested before it settles).
**Disinterestedness** is the motivation guard behind the book's own "the cook never tastes the dish":
the speaker's stake is discounted because the institution assumes motivated belief, not malice.

The norms come with named **counternorms** (Mitroff 1974, studying Apollo scientists): universalism is
opposed by *particularism* — "assess new knowledge based on the reputation and past productivity of
the individual or research group"; organized skepticism by *organized dogmatism*. The counternorm is
literally "settle by authority/reputation," which is what the chapter exists to refuse.

**Citations.**
- Merton, Robert K. (1942), "Science and Technology in a Democratic Order," *Journal of Legal and
  Political Sociology* 1: 115–126; reprinted as "The Normative Structure of Science" in Merton,
  *The Sociology of Science: Theoretical and Empirical Investigations* (Univ. of Chicago Press, 1973),
  pp. 267–278. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mertonian_norms (full-text reprint:
  https://sciencepolicy.colorado.edu/students/envs_5110/merton_sociology_science.pdf )
- Mitroff, Ian I. (1974), "Norms and Counter-Norms in a Select Group of the Apollo Moon Scientists,"
  *American Sociological Review* 39(4): 579–595. doi:10.2307/2094423 (counternorms, including
  particularism as the opposite of universalism).
- Anderson, Melissa S. et al. (2010), "Extending the Mertonian Norms: Scientists' Subscription to
  Norms of Research," *Journal of Higher Education* 81(3): 366–393. PMC2995462 (empirical study of
  whether scientists actually endorse the four norms).
- Macfarlane, Bruce (2024), "The DECAY of Merton's scientific norms and the new academic ethos,"

**What it adds to the chapter.** Science itself wrote down "settle on impersonal evidence, not on the
status of the speaker" as its institutional ethos over eighty years ago, named the four parts, and
named the counternorm (judging by reputation) that the chapter is refusing. The book's rule is not a
private invention; it is universalism-plus-organized-skepticism stated for a kitchen.

### 2.3 Peer review and Fagan inspection — the 360-year-old rule that someone who didn't make it must judge it

**The practice (science).** The institutional pattern — *a work is not accepted until an independent
judge, who did not produce it, has examined it* — is older than any living laboratory. On **6 March
1665** the Royal Society launched *Philosophical Transactions*, the world's first scientific journal,
under the editorship of its Secretary **Henry Oldenburg**, who is credited as the "father" of modern
scientific peer review for the practice of referring submissions to independent fellows. The
*practice* of refereeing is thus 360 years old; the *term* "peer review" is not — historians (Csiszar,
Baldwin, Moxham & Fyfe) show it was coined only in the late 1960s / early 1970s, and mandatory
external peer review as a universal standard is a post-WWII / Cold-War institutionalization (*Nature*
made it standard practice in 1973), tied to the expansion of government-funded science. So: refereeing
is ancient; the name and the universal mandate are recent; the underlying rule — independent judgment
before acceptance — is the constant.

**The practice (software).** In 1976 **Michael E. Fagan** of IBM transferred the same institutional
rule from hardware inspection to software: a *structured* review with assigned roles (moderator,
author, reader/inspectors), in which the people judging the code are **not** the people who wrote it.
The effectiveness numbers are in the first batch (Fagan ~82% of defects; broader range 20–93%); the
point here is the *institutional transfer*: Fagan's contribution was to codify, for a new domain, that
the maker never approves their own work and that the inspection is a *separate, role-assigned pass*,
not a casual re-read.

**The through-line.** *1665 Royal Society referees → 1970s formal "peer review" → 1976 Fagan
inspection.* Three institutions, three centuries apart, one rule: the work is judged by someone who
did not produce it, before it is allowed to leave.

lineage, not proposing a novelty.

### 2.4 Devil's advocate / institutionalized dissent — because lone dissent is fragile, charter a dissenter

**The practice.** The first batch's measured finding (Asch) is that a *single* independent dissenter
collapses conformity error from ~37% to ~5%. The institutional lesson across very different fields is
the same: **do not hope a dissenter speaks up — create the role, give it a charter, and protect it.**
The institution supplies the independence the lone individual cannot guarantee.

- **Catholic Church — the *Advocatus Diaboli* (1587).** Pope **Sixtus V** formally established the
  office in 1587 within the Sacred Congregation of Rites. Its official title was ***Promotor Fidei***
  (Promoter of the Faith); the popular name "Devil's Advocate" came from its assigned job: to argue
  *against* the canonization of a candidate, marshalling every skeptical objection to the alleged
  miracles and virtues. The role was reduced in influence by **John Paul II's 1983** reform of the
  canonization process, but its 400-year function is the point: the institution *staffed* the
  skeptic's seat so that sainthood could not settle on enthusiasm or rank alone.
- **US intelligence — CIA "Team B" (1976).** In 1976, outgoing CIA Director **George H. W. Bush**
  authorized a "competitive analysis" experiment: an outside team of experts (**Team B**, headed by
  Harvard historian Richard Pipes) independently assessed Soviet strategic intentions, separately from
  the CIA's own in-house analysts (**Team A**). Roberta and Albert Wohlstetter were part of the
  intellectual pressure (via the President's Foreign Intelligence Advisory Board) that created it. The
  structural innovation is the point — two teams with *independent incentives* produced two
  assessments and let the evidence arbitrate between them. (Team B is also a cautionary tale: critics
  later argued it overestimated Soviet capabilities, which is itself a proof-over-authority lesson — a
  designated dissenter can still be wrong, so its verdict is *evidence*, not *proof*.)
- **NASA — the red team and the NESC (2003).** The "red team" concept — a designated group whose job
  is to play the adversary and challenge the plan — originated in the **US military during the Cold
  War** (early 1960s, rooted in wargaming and game theory). NASA's institutionalized form is the
  **NASA Engineering and Safety Center (NESC)**, founded in **2003** at Langley Research Center on the
  direct recommendation of the **Columbia Accident Investigation Board (CAIB)**, which concluded that
  NASA's *culture and organization* had as much to do with the Columbia disaster as the physical
  cause. The NESC is an **Independent Technical Authority** — explicitly insulated from program-level
  pressure — performing "independent testing, analysis, and assessments of NASA's high-risk projects."
  The institutional response to "dissent was suppressed and people died" was to *build the dissenter
  into the org chart*.

**The common form.** Asch measured that one dissenter is worth it; the Catholic Church in 1587, the
CIA in 1976, and NASA in 2003 each concluded the same thing — that you cannot rely on a dissenter
*volunteering*, so you charter one. The chapter's clause requiring confirmation "by someone
independent of BOTH the author and the owner" is the kitchen-scale version of the same structural move.

**What it adds to the chapter.** The rule's requirement of an *independent confirmer* is not a
novelty; it is the kitchen version of a move institutions have made for 400 years when the stakes are
high — *charter a protected dissenter*, because volunteered dissent is too fragile to bet on. The
chapter is in the company of canonization, intelligence analysis, and mission safety, not alone.

## Synthesis — what the second batch adds to the whole

Taken with the first batch, the prior-art facet now makes four claims the chapter can lean on:

1. **A mature profession installs one supreme rule** (engineering paramountcy). The book's having a
   top invariant is not a quirk; it is the engineering move.
2. **Science codified "settle on evidence, not status" 80+ years ago** (Merton's universalism +
   organized skepticism), and named "judging by reputation" as the counternorm to refuse.
3. **The "independent judge" is a 360-year-old, cross-domain pattern** (Royal Society 1665 → Fagan
   1976). The book's "taster is never the cook" is the same rule for dishes and chapters.
4. **Because lone dissent is fragile, real institutions charter a dissenter** (1587 / 1976 / 2003).
   The rule's "independent confirmer" is the kitchen version of that structural move.

The chapter's "settle by proof, not authority" is therefore **convention with a long pedigree**, not
an invention — which is the strongest framing the book can give a rule it wants readers to treat as
load-bearing.
