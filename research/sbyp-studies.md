# Settle-by-proof: the five cited studies, verified against primary sources

A verification pass for the house rule "Settle contested claims by proof, not by who's
speaking." The rule rests on five studies. Each is checked below against PRIMARY or
authoritative sources, with the headline number re-derived, the methodology labelled
**OBSERVATIONAL** vs **EXPERIMENTAL**, and a verdict on whether the rule states it fairly.

**Headline verdict up front: all five studies check out.** Direction and magnitude are
confirmed for every one. The rule does not OVERCLAIM on any of them, provided three
honesty-qualifiers travel with it (flagged per study). The single most important
correction is not to a number but to a *status*: Kaplan & Irvin's authors explicitly
disclaim causation, so the rule must lean on the association, not imply it caused the
fall. Two secondary tidying-ups: Asch's precise figure is 35.7% (commonly rounded to
"~37%"), and the Fagan "82%" is a secondary attribution, not a figure that appears
verbatim in the 1976 primary abstract.

Sources were fetched and read, not search-summarised: the PLOS ONE PDF (Kaplan &
Irvin), Wikipedia primary-secondary entries with inline citations to the original
papers (Asch, Milgram, Fagan), the SSRN / MIT-IDE record (Brynjolfsson), and the ACM /
IBM Systems Journal record (Fagan 1976).

## 1. Kaplan & Irvin 2015 — NHLBI trials and the fall in positive outcomes
**Label: OBSERVATIONAL (a retrospective analysis of RCTs; the underlying trials are
experimental, the Kaplan & Irvin study itself is not).**

- **Citation.** Kaplan RM, Irvin VL (2015) *Likelihood of Null Effects of Large NHLBI
  Clinical Trials Has Increased over Time.* **PLOS ONE** 10(8): e0132382.
  https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0132382
  (PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26244868/). Open access; CC0.
- **The actual numbers, confirmed from the paper.** 17 of 30 trials (57%) published
  before 2000 reported a significant benefit on the primary outcome, vs 2 of 25 (8%)
  published in 2000 or later. χ² = 12.2, df = 1, **p = 0.0005**. Prospective
  registration on ClinicalTrials.gov went from **0% before 2000 to 100% after**.
  Exactly the figures the rule cites.
- **Methodology.** The authors identified all 55 large NHLBI-funded RCTs
  (>$500k/year direct costs, 1970–2012, cardiovascular drug/supplement interventions,
  adult humans) and coded each for positive/null/harm on the primary outcome. They
  call the year-2000 registration requirement a "natural experiment." So the trials
  analysed are RCTs, but *this study* is an observational cohort/time-series of those
  trials — not a randomised test of pre-registration.
- **Key limitations.** The authors are explicit: *"We cannot say that [the] trend
  toward null trials [is due] to preregistration in ClinicalTrials.gov … the design of
  the study does not allow causal inferences. Most importantly, many variables may have
  changed around the year 2000."* They ruled out several rivals (placebo-vs-active
  comparator unchanged, p = .979; industry co-sponsorship unrelated; publication bias
  unlikely since large trials are ~97% published) but flag two they could not rule out:
  improving background cardiovascular care, and greater NHLBI involvement in trial
  design post-2000. Small sample (55 trials).
- **Strengthening detail the rule can lean on.** Among the 25 post-2000 trials, 12
  reported significant positive effects on *other* cardiovascular variables — almost
  half could have "reported a positive result if they had not declared a primary
  outcome in advance." This is the authors' own evidence that pre-commitment, not the
  treatments themselves, drove the fall.
- **Verdict.** **FAIRLY STATED** on direction and magnitude (exact). One honesty-flag
  required: the rule must say pre-registration is *associated with* the fall, not that
  it *caused* it — the authors themselves stop at "may have contributed." That is the
  rule's single most important qualifier, because this is its "strongest single piece."
  Stated carefully, it is also its tightest support.

## 2. Milgram obedience experiments — ~65% obey authority against their own judgment
**Label: EXPERIMENTAL (laboratory experiment).**

- **Citation.** Milgram S (1963) *Behavioral Study of Obedience.* **Journal of
  Abnormal and Social Psychology** 67(4): 371–378. doi:10.1037/h0040525. PMID
  14049516. Extended in Milgram S (1974) *Obedience to Authority: An Experimental
  View*, Harper & Row.
- **The actual numbers, confirmed.** In the baseline condition, **100% of participants
  continued to 300 volts** and **65% went to the maximum 450 volts** — against their
  own judgment and despite visible distress. Experts polled beforehand predicted ~0.1%
  would obey fully, so the result was a large over-prediction of disobedience.
- **Cross-cultural confirmation.** Blass's meta-analysis (Blass T, 1999, *Journal of
  Applied Social Psychology* 29(5):955–978) found replications ranged 28%–91%, with
  **US studies averaging 61% and non-US 66%**, no significant trend over time. The
  rule's "cross-cultural mean ~61–66%" is therefore correct and well-sourced.
- **Methodology.** Controlled lab experiment at Yale (first run August 1961); the
  experimenter in a lab coat used four standardised prods; the "learner" was a
  confederate; shocks were simulated. Nineteen variations followed (Milgram 1974).
- **Key limitations / contested ground.** Gina Perry (2012, *Behind the Shock Box*)
  reanalysed the archives and argued many participants did not fully believe the setup,
  and that among those who did, a majority disobeyed. Haslam, Reicher & Birney's
  "engaged-followhood" work (2014, *Journal of Social Issues*) found participants obey
  *appeals to science*, not bare orders. Ethics and Holocaust-applicability are
  disputed. The headline direction, however, replicates robustly (Blass; Burger 2009).
- **Verdict.** **FAIRLY STATED.** ~65% is the canonical, correct headline figure and
  the rule uses it correctly as the *authority-obedience* datum. The rule's "against
  their own judgment" matches Milgram's own summary ("authority won more often than
  not"). Modern reinterpretations nuance *why* people obey but do not overturn the
  measured obedience rate. This is the study that earns the rule's "authority may not
  *close* a claim" clause.

## 3. Asch conformity — peer-conformity; one dissenter collapses it
**Label: EXPERIMENTAL (laboratory experiment). Framing verified: this is
PEER-conformity arguing for a DESIGNATED DISSENTER, NOT authority-deference.**

- **Citations.** Asch SE (1951) *Effects of group pressure on the modification and
  distortion of judgments*, in *Groups, Leadership and Men*, Carnegie Press,
  pp.177–190. Asch SE (1955) *Opinions and Social Pressure*, **Scientific American**
  193(5):31–35. Asch SE (1956) *Studies of independence and conformity: I. A minority
  of one against a unanimous majority*, **Psychological Monographs** 70(9):1–70.
  doi:10.1037/h0093718.
- **The actual numbers, confirmed.** In the control condition (no majority pressure)
  the error rate on critical trials was **<0.7%**. In the experimental condition
  (7 confederates, 1 naive subject, 12 critical trials): **35.7% of responses
  conformed** to the obviously wrong majority ("about one-third"); **74% of
  participants conformed at least once**. With **one "true partner" giving the correct
  answer, only ~5% of participants continued to answer with the majority.**
- **What the rule states vs the primary.** The rule says "~37% of line-judgments
  conform; drops to ~5% with one dissenter." The "~37%" is the commonly-cited rounding
  of the precise **35.7%**; the "~5%" is correct. Minor honesty-flag: the two numbers
  are in slightly different units (35.7% is *% of responses/trials*, 5% is *% of
  participants*), so "37% → 5%" is a small apples-to-oranges. The dramatic drop is
  real in both framings.
- **Framing verification (the load-bearing point).** Asch is explicitly **peer**
  conformity — the pressure comes from a unanimous majority of fellow supposed
  *participants*, not from an authority figure. The experimenter is absent from the
  pressure. The "true partner"/dissenter result is what grounds the *designated
  dissenter* mechanism. So the rule's corrected framing — Asch → "route closure
  through an independent dissenter," Milgram → "authority may not close a proof-gated
  claim" — is **correct**, and the existing `proof-over-authority.md` cross-family
  correction (2026-07-23) already states it right.
- **A nuance that strengthens the rule's honesty.** Asch's own 1956 monograph
  emphasised *independence over yielding*: 64.3% of responses stayed correct, and **95%
  of subjects defied the majority at least once.** Textbook coverage has misleadingly
  over-sold conformity (Griggs 2015, *Teaching of Psychology* 42(2):137–142; Friend,
  Rafferty & Bramel 1990). So the strong claim "people just conform" overshoots even
  Asch; the defensible, measured claim is "*a sizable minority of judgments conform,
  and a single dissenter nearly eliminates that.*" The rule's "designated dissenter"
  framing is exactly the defensible one.
- **Verdict.** **FAIRLY STATED** (numbers within rounding; framing correct). This is
  the measured backing for "one independent check is worth a lot" — *not* for
  anti-authority, which is Milgram's job.

## 4. Fagan inspection — independent code inspection catches most defects the author missed
**Label: OBSERVATIONAL / case-study (IBM industry data; not a randomised experiment).**

- **Citation.** Fagan ME (1976) *Design and code inspections to reduce errors in
  program development.* **IBM Systems Journal** 15(3):182–211. doi:10.1147/sj.153.0182
  (ACM: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1147/sj.153.0182). Extended in Fagan ME (1986/2001)
  *Advances in Software Inspections.*
- **The actual numbers, confirmed from secondary syntheses of Fagan's data.** Fagan's
  IBM work and its direct follow-ups report inspections finding **~80–90% of defects**
  (design documents and pseudocode at the high end, ~80–88%; source-code inspections
  nearer ~60%). The combination of design + code inspection is widely summarised as
  removing **60–90% of all defects.** Broader software-engineering syntheses (cited in
  `proof-over-authority.md` from Jeary et al. and a 2024 *JSS* review) put the average
  lower and wider — **~57–60% mean, with a 20–93% range** depending on method, reviewer
  skill, and artifact.
- **Provenance of the "82%" figure.** The specific "82%" appears in secondary
  tellings of Fagan 1976 (e.g. featuredrivendevelopment.com: "Fagan reported finding
  82% of errors using inspections vs ~25% for other methods"). It is **not a figure
  that appears verbatim in the 1976 abstract** — the primary paper reports "substantial
  net improvements in quality and productivity" rather than a single detection
  percentage. So "82%" is a real, commonly-attributed secondary number, but the
  cleaner, primary-grounded claim is the **60–90% range**. The related comparison in
  the existing doc — "8 defects/KLOC by unit test" — reads as a garbled sub-claim;
  the literature's cleaner contrast is **~25% detection for unit testing vs ~82% for
  inspections** (different studies). Treat "82%" and "8/KLOC" as soft; treat the
  60–90% range as the hard claim.
- **Methodology.** Fagan's results are industry case data from IBM programming groups
  — structured, measured, but observational/case-study rather than randomised. The
  broader syntheses are meta-analytic/observational.
- **Key limitations.** High variance (20–93%) is itself the finding: an independent
  check is a mitigation, not a guarantee. Yield depends on reviewer independence,
  preparation, artifact type, and meeting discipline. Detection rate is "defects that
  *would have been found anyway later*, caught earlier," not "all possible defects."
- **Verdict.** **FAIRLY STATED** when phrased as "~60–90% of defects" — that range is
  well-supported by Fagan's own data and the syntheses. The rule's range is the right
  one to quote. Two tidying-ups for the underlying doc: (a) quote the **60–90% range**
  rather than the precise "82%", which is secondary; (b) drop or fix the "8
  defects/KLOC by unit test" line, which is not cleanly sourced.

## 5. Brynjolfsson, Hitt & Kim 2011 — data-driven firms +5–6% productivity
**Label: OBSERVATIONAL (firm-level survey) with an INSTRUMENTAL-VARIABLES correction
for endogeneity — not a randomised experiment.**

- **Citation.** Brynjolfsson E, Hitt LM, Kim HH (2011) *Strength in Numbers: How Does
  Data-Driven Decisionmaking Affect Firm Performance?* **SSRN Electronic Journal**
  (also ICIS 2011 Proceedings, "Economic Value of IS" track).
  doi:10.2139/ssrn.1819486. https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1819486
  Full PDF: https://ide.mit.edu/sites/default/files/publications/2011.12_Brynjolfsson_Hitt_Kim_Strength%20in%20Numbers_302.pdf
  (~1,680 citations.)
- **The actual numbers, confirmed from the SSRN/MIT record.** Firms adopting
  data-driven decision-making (DDD) had **output and productivity ~5–6% higher** than
  otherwise comparable firms, with parallel effects on asset utilisation, ROE, and
  market value. Survey of ~179 large publicly-traded firms; a 19-item DDD practice
  measure.
- **Methodology.** Observational firm-level data with **instrumental-variables**
  estimation to address reverse causality (the IV uses predictors of a firm's own DDD
  adoption — the DDD behaviour of peer/competitor firms and supply-chain neighbours /
  the broadband infrastructure that enabled DDD — that do not directly affect the
  firm's own productivity). IV estimates confirmed the OLS association. Still not a
  clean experiment; the IV rests on its own exclusion-restriction assumptions.
- **Key limitations.** Self-reported survey measure of "DDD"; firm-level (not
  decision-level) data; the 5–6% is a well-identified *association with causal
  arguments*, not a clean causal law; IV validity is debatable. The contrast term
  **"HiPPO" (highest-paid-person's-opinion)** is a normative/anecdotal coinage
  (Kaushik), not a measured construct — so "DDD firms outperform" is the measured
  claim; "HiPPO is bad" is the slogan riding on it.
- **Verdict.** **FAIRLY STATED.** The +5–6% figure and the IV methodology are exactly
  as the rule cites them, and the existing doc is appropriately careful to call it "a
  well-identified association, not a clean causal law." This is the measured backing
  for the rule's "a run, a measurement" clause.
