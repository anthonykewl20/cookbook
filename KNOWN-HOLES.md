# Known holes

The reason this book exists is to close these, not to list them and live with them. A hole
that is written down is being worked on. A hole nobody wrote down is the one that takes the
building down.

**A hole may not be left open in a published chapter.** If a chapter ships, its holes are
closed — or the chapter says plainly what it does not cover and where that is handled
instead.

## Closed

| Hole | How it was closed |
|---|---|
| At the smallest scale the cook would have to approve their own plate | The taster is a **base role**, present from day one. The floor is six roles, and no version below it ships. Nobody ever checks their own work. |
| The owner cannot personally manage a head chef in every restaurant; attention runs out long before money does | **The area manager** — a chain-level role that holds the head chefs. It must run without the owner in the loop, or it has saved no attention. |
| The head chef and the taster both look at the dish — two checks or one, and where is the line? | **Different questions, not different depths.** The head chef asks whether the process was followed; the taster asks whether the quality is right; the taster's word is final. The doctor later became the third: whether the people and the building are still fit. Method, result, means — no overlap. |
| Nobody's job was to notice a worker or the building failing. The head chef checks the method and the taster checks the result; a cook slowly getting worse shows up in neither until a dish is already bad | **The doctor** — a base role that reads the log for decline, diagnoses at once rather than weekly, and names what is wrong rather than that something is. It never treats what it finds. |
| Is the prep cook a base role or a growth role? | **Base.** The cook doing their own grunt work is what makes a dish slow. Slicing and peeling belongs to someone else from day one. Seven base roles, not six. |
| Is a recipe fixed by the restaurant that found it wrong, or only by the owner? Consistency dies if each kitchen edits its own; the owner's attention dies if every fix waits for them | **Raised from below, changed at the top, amended once.** Any employee may raise a fault; nobody in a kitchen may amend the manual. The owner decides — or the assistant acting for them — and the corrected recipe goes to every restaurant at once. |
| The taster reads the head chef's verdict before giving its own, which is how a second check becomes a rubber stamp | **Closed by separating the questions, not the order.** The head chef answers *was the process followed*; the taster answers *is the quality right*. Reading one answer cannot supply the other. Standing rule: the taster gives a quality verdict on every dish, and may never reason "the process was followed, so the plate must be fine." |

## Open

| Hole | Where it gets closed |
|---|---|
| With one of each role, a single absence stops service. Cover is a growth answer, but the strain appears at the smallest scale. | Part Three, when it goes wrong — or Part Four, cover |
| The owner is not in the kitchen, so nothing is known upstairs unless someone downstairs writes it down. A fault seen and not recorded is a fault the chain never had a chance to fix. | Part Three, ch. 25 — saying something is wrong |
| **The floor keeps rising.** Every role added to the base makes the smallest possible restaurant bigger, and a floor set too high means nobody can open one. Eight roles now, up from four. Each one has earned its place — but nothing is currently checking the total. | Part Three intro, before it is written |
